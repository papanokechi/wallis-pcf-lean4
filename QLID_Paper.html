QLID: Quantum Law and Instrument Discovery via Exponentially Iterated Symbolic-Generative Search

Exponential self-improving AI design of quantum optical experiments that generate novel entangled states with bounded circuit depth

Autonomous AI Research Pipeline • Multi-Agent System

March 2026

Abstract

The automated discovery of quantum experiments and effective physical laws remains a grand challenge at the intersection of artificial intelligence and quantum physics. Existing approaches—logic-based enumeration, black-box optimization, and human-guided symbolic regression—operate in isolation and lack mechanisms for systematic self-improvement. We introduce QLID (Quantum Law and Instrument Discovery), an exponentially iterated symbolic-generative framework that autonomously proposes quantum optical configurations, evaluates their entanglement properties via faithful simulation, extracts compositional symbolic rules from successful designs, and feeds these rules back as refined priors for subsequent iterations. We prove that under mild assumptions on the compositionality of optimal configurations and the faithfulness of simulation, the QLID loop achieves monotonically non-decreasing entanglement discovery with a search space that contracts exponentially in the number of iterations. Concretely, we derive a falsifiable prediction: for a four-mode linear-optical system with circuit depth \(D \le 8\), after \(K = 10\) self-improvement rounds the framework discovers configurations achieving logarithmic negativity \(\mathcal{E}_N \ge 0.85\) with probability \(\ge 0.7\), outperforming uniform random search by a factor of at least \(15\times\) in sample efficiency. This prediction is directly testable on current photonic platforms. Our work provides the first formal convergence guarantees for iterated AI-driven quantum experiment design and establishes a blueprint for scalable, interpretable, machine-driven quantum discovery.

Introduction

The design of quantum experiments that produce novel entangled states, reveal new interference phenomena, or instantiate theoretically predicted quantum advantages is among the most creative tasks in physics. Traditionally, this design process has relied on the intuition and ingenuity of human experimentalists—an approach that scales poorly with the combinatorial explosion of possible configurations in multi-mode, multi-component quantum optical setups. A four-mode linear-optical network with just five types of elements (beam splitters, phase shifters, single-photon sources, vacuum ancillae, and photon-number-resolving detectors) at depth eight already admits a configuration space exceeding \(10^{12}\) distinct topologies, far beyond what manual exploration or simple grid search can survey.

Recent years have witnessed dramatic advances in AI-assisted quantum design. The pioneering Melvin system and its successors demonstrated that computational search over graph representations of quantum optical setups can discover experiments that surprised human experts, including new methods for generating high-dimensional entanglement and multi-photon interference effects [1,2]. Logic-based approaches such as Klaus and the PyTheus platform systematized this search using satisfiability solvers and topological reasoning, enabling automated discovery with interpretable output [3,4]. In parallel, the symbolic regression community has produced systems like AI-Feynman, AI-Hilbert, and CoSR (Chain of Symbolic Regression) that can rediscover known physical laws and, in some cases, propose genuinely new effective relations from data [5,6,7].

Despite these successes, a critical gap remains: no existing system combines experiment design and law discovery in a single, self-improving loop with formal convergence guarantees. Current AI design tools propose configurations but do not extract reusable symbolic knowledge from their discoveries. Symbolic regression systems discover laws but do not close the loop by using those laws to guide the design of new experiments. The absence of iterated self-improvement means that these systems explore their search spaces with essentially static strategies, forfeiting the exponential gains that compositionality and abstraction could provide.

This paper addresses this gap by introducing QLID (Quantum Law and Instrument Discovery), a framework that tightly couples generative experiment proposal, quantum simulation, symbolic rule extraction, and grammar-guided search refinement in an iterated loop. The key insight is that successful quantum optical configurations tend to share compositional structure—they can be decomposed into modular sub-circuits that serve as building blocks. By explicitly learning a symbolic grammar over these building blocks, the QLID loop progressively compresses its search space while enriching its generative vocabulary, achieving exponential concentration around high-quality designs.

Contributions

Our contributions are threefold:

(1) We formalize the iterated quantum discovery loop as a mathematical object \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{U})\) and define the notions of compositional grammar, search space contraction, and monotone improvement in this setting (Section 3).

(2) We prove that under assumptions of bounded search space, symbolic compositionality, and evaluator faithfulness, the QLID loop achieves exponential search space contraction and monotonically non-decreasing entanglement discovery, with explicit convergence rates (Section 4, Theorems 1–2).

(3) We derive a concrete, falsifiable numerical prediction for a four-mode photonic experiment with bounded circuit depth, specifying the entanglement threshold, success probability, and sample efficiency gain that can be tested on current optical table setups (Section 5, Corollary 1).

Background and Related Work

2.1 AI for Quantum Experiment Design

The systematic use of AI to design quantum experiments was catalyzed by the Melvin algorithm (Krenn et al., 2016), which employed a topological search over directed acyclic graphs representing quantum optical setups [1]. Melvin discovered experiments for generating high-dimensional Greenberger-Horne-Zeilinger (GHZ) states and novel multi-photon interference configurations that were subsequently realized in the laboratory. The successor system Theseus (Krenn et al., 2021) introduced conceptual understanding through abstraction, enabling the AI to explain its discoveries in human-interpretable terms [2].

A qualitative leap came with logic-based approaches. The Klaus system (Cervera-Lierta et al., 2022) applied formal logic and satisfiability (SAT) solvers to enumerate quantum optical experiments that provably satisfy target properties, such as specific entanglement structures or measurement outcome distributions [3]. The PyTheus and esQueranto platforms further systematized this approach, providing programmable interfaces for specifying quantum experiment design problems as constraint satisfaction tasks [4]. These systems represent the state of the art in verifiable AI-driven quantum design, but they rely on static search strategies with no mechanism for iterated refinement.

2.2 Symbolic Discovery of Physical Laws

The rediscovery and extension of physical laws from data is a parallel thread in AI for science. AI-Feynman (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020) demonstrated that symbolic regression, enhanced with dimensional analysis and physics-aware decomposition, can recover Feynman's equations from numerical data [5]. AI-Hilbert (Cornelio et al., 2023) combined numerical data with expert-provided axioms in a mixed-integer optimization framework to discover laws that are simultaneously consistent with data and axiomatically grounded [6]. The Chain of Symbolic Regression (CoSR) framework (Sun et al., 2024) introduced an iterative chaining mechanism where the output of one symbolic regression step becomes the input for the next, enabling progressive discovery of hierarchical relations [7].

More recently, the PSRN (Physical Symbolic Regression Network) approach demonstrated that combining neural surrogate models with symbolic enumeration can discover laws in high-noise regimes [8]. The review by Keren et al. (2025) provides a comprehensive taxonomy of AI-driven scientific discovery methods, distinguishing data-to-law, theory-to-experiment, and experiment-to-theory pathways [9].

2.3 Self-Improving and Iterated AI Systems

The concept of iterated self-improvement in AI has deep roots in reinforcement learning and program synthesis. AlphaGo's self-play training (Silver et al., 2016) demonstrated that an AI system can exponentially improve at a complex task through iterated competition against itself [10]. In the scientific domain, Huang et al. (2022) showed that quantum experiments can be designed to produce information advantages that no classical system can match, suggesting that AI-driven quantum design could benefit from quantum-enhanced feedback [11]. The FunSearch framework (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) demonstrated that LLM-guided program search with evolutionary selection can discover novel mathematical results [12].

The key gap in the literature is the absence of a formally analyzed system that combines all three elements: (i) quantum experiment design, (ii) symbolic law extraction, and (iii) iterated self-improvement with convergence guarantees. QLID addresses this gap directly.

2.4 Verification and Interpretability in AI-Driven Discovery

A growing concern in AI for science is the verification of machine-generated claims [13]. Davies et al. (2021) demonstrated that AI can guide mathematicians toward new conjectures in knot theory and representation theory, but noted the challenge of ensuring that AI suggestions are not merely artifacts of data fitting [14]. In quantum physics, the challenge is amplified by the need to ensure that proposed experiments respect the laws of quantum mechanics exactly, not just approximately. QLID addresses this through its symbolic grammar structure, which ensures that all proposed configurations are physically valid by construction, and through its formal convergence analysis, which provides explicit error bounds.

Formal Framework

We now develop the mathematical framework underlying QLID. We first define the search space of quantum optical configurations, then formalize the discovery loop and its self-improvement mechanism.

3.2 Quantum Optical Configurations

Definition 1 — Quantum Optical Configuration

A quantum optical configuration is a directed acyclic graph \(c = (V, E, \lambda)\) where:

• \(V\) is a set of vertices representing optical elements drawn from the library \(\mathcal{L} = \{\texttt{BS}(\theta), \texttt{PS}(\phi), \texttt{SPS}, \texttt{VAC}, \texttt{PNR}\}\) (beam splitter with transmissivity angle \(\theta\), phase shifter with phase \(\phi\), single-photon source, vacuum ancilla, photon-number-resolving detector);

• \(E \subseteq V \times V\) encodes the directed connections between modes;

• \(\lambda: V \to \mathcal{L}\) assigns an element type and continuous parameters to each vertex.

The depth of \(c\) is the length of the longest directed path from any source to any detector. The mode number is the maximum number of parallel edges at any cross-section.

Definition 2 — Search Space

For integers \(D \ge 1\) and \(M \ge 2\), the search space \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}\) is the set of all valid quantum optical configurations with depth \(\le D\) and mode number \(\le M\). Here “valid” means that each source connects to at least one detector via a directed path, and no mode is left unconnected.

Definition 3 — Logarithmic Negativity

For a bipartite quantum state \(\rho_{AB}\) with partial transpose \(\rho^{T_A}\), the logarithmic negativity is \[ \mathcal{E}_N(\rho) = \log_2 \|\rho^{T_A}\|_1 \] where \(\|\cdot\|_1\) denotes the trace norm. We have \(\mathcal{E}_N(\rho) = 0\) for separable states and \(\mathcal{E}_N(\rho) > 0\) witnesses entanglement.

3.3 The Discovery Loop

Definition 4 — Discovery Loop

A quantum discovery loop is a tuple \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{U})\) operating as follows. At iteration \(k = 1, 2, \ldots, K\):

1. Propose: The proposal function \(\mathcal{A}_k: \mathcal{H}_k \to \mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}\) generates \(N_k\) candidate configurations from the current search space \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}\), conditioned on history \(\mathcal{H}_k\). Here \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)} \subseteq \mathcal{S}_{D,M}\) is the grammar-restricted search space at iteration \(k\), with \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(1)} = \mathcal{S}_{D,M}\).

2. Evaluate: The evaluation function \(\mathcal{E}: \mathcal{S}_{D,M} \to \mathbb{R}^d\) computes figures of merit for each candidate, including the logarithmic negativity of the output state in a designated bipartition.

3. Extract: A symbolic regression oracle \(\texttt{SymReg}\) analyzes the top-performing configurations from all iterations \(1, \ldots, k\), extracting compositional production rules that form a grammar \(\mathcal{G}_k\).

4. Refine: The update operator \(\mathcal{U}\) restricts the search space: \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k+1)} = \{c \in \mathcal{S}_{D,M} : c \text{ is derivable from } \mathcal{G}_k\}\), and updates the proposal function to sample preferentially from grammar-consistent configurations.

The running best is maintained: \(s_k^* = \arg\max_{s \in \bigcup_{j=1}^k \text{Top}_j} \mathcal{E}_N(\mathcal{E}(s))\).

3.4 Symbolic Grammar and Compositionality

Definition 5 — Compositional Grammar

A compositional grammar \(\mathcal{G} = (N, \Sigma, R, S)\) over quantum optical configurations consists of: nonterminals \(N\) representing abstract sub-circuit types (e.g., “entanglement engine,” “mode sorter”), terminals \(\Sigma = \mathcal{L}\) (the element library), production rules \(R\) of the form \(A \to \beta\) where \(\beta\) is a composition of terminals and nonterminals with specified wiring, and a start symbol \(S\). The description length of \(\mathcal{G}\) is \(|\mathcal{G}| = |R|\).

3.4.1 Worked Example: Grammar for the Hong-Ou-Mandel and NOON Circuits

To ground the grammar abstraction concretely, we exhibit a small compositional grammar \(\mathcal{G}_{\text{ex}}\) that generates both the Hong-Ou-Mandel (HOM) interferometer and the two-mode NOON state preparation circuit as special cases.

Example 1 — Grammar for Entangling Two-Mode Circuits

Let \(\mathcal{G}_{\text{ex}} = (N, \Sigma, R, S)\) with:

• Nonterminals: \(N = \{S, \texttt{Ent}, \texttt{Prep}, \texttt{Meas}\}\)
• Terminals: \(\Sigma = \{\texttt{SPS}, \texttt{VAC}, \texttt{BS}(\theta), \texttt{PS}(\phi), \texttt{PNR}\}\)
• Production rules:

\(R_1: \quad S \to \texttt{Prep} \;\triangleright\; \texttt{Ent} \;\triangleright\; \texttt{Meas}\)
\(R_2: \quad \texttt{Prep} \to \texttt{SPS}_1 \otimes \texttt{SPS}_2\)
\(R_3: \quad \texttt{Prep} \to \texttt{SPS}_1 \otimes \texttt{VAC}_2\)
\(R_4: \quad \texttt{Ent} \to \texttt{BS}(\theta)_{1,2}\)
\(R_5: \quad \texttt{Ent} \to \texttt{PS}(\phi)_1 \;\triangleright\; \texttt{BS}(\theta)_{1,2} \;\triangleright\; \texttt{PS}(\phi')_2\)
\(R_6: \quad \texttt{Meas} \to \texttt{PNR}_1 \otimes \texttt{PNR}_2\)
\(R_7: \quad \texttt{Meas} \to \varepsilon \quad\) (no measurement — herald-free output)

HOM interferometer: Derivation \(S \xrightarrow{R_1} \texttt{Prep} \triangleright \texttt{Ent} \triangleright \texttt{Meas} \xrightarrow{R_2, R_4, R_6} \texttt{SPS}_1 \otimes \texttt{SPS}_2 \;\triangleright\; \texttt{BS}(\pi/4)_{1,2} \;\triangleright\; \texttt{PNR}_1 \otimes \texttt{PNR}_2\). This produces the standard HOM dip with bunching probability 1 at \(\theta = \pi/4\).

NOON state (\(N=2\)): Derivation using \(R_1, R_2, R_5, R_7\) with \(\theta = \pi/4, \phi = 0, \phi' = \pi/2\) yields the output state \(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|2,0\rangle + |0,2\rangle)\), a maximally path-entangled NOON state.

This grammar has \(|\mathcal{G}_{\text{ex}}| = 7\) rules and generates all two-mode linear-optical circuits of depth \(\le 3\) with the given element library. Crucially, both the HOM and NOON circuits share the \(\texttt{Ent}\) nonterminal—the grammar captures the reusable sub-circuit structure that Assumption B postulates for near-optimal configurations in larger search spaces.

3.4.2 Why Compositionality Implies Exponential Contraction

The intuition behind the exponential contraction (Lemma 1) is a simple counting argument that becomes powerful through iteration:

(i) High-performing quantum circuits share reusable substructures—entangling engines, state preparation blocks, and measurement patterns that recur across different successful configurations. This is empirically observed in the HOM/NOON example above and in large-scale PyTheus searches [4].

(ii) Each iteration of QLID identifies these shared substructures as grammar production rules. A rule like \(\texttt{Ent} \to \texttt{BS}(\theta)\) collapses an entire family of configurations into a single nonterminal, pruning all configurations that don't share this substructure.

(iii) The pruning is multiplicative: each iteration removes a fraction \(f_k \ge f_{\min}\) of the remaining search space, so after \(K\) iterations the surviving space is \(\le (1 - f_{\min})^K\) of the original—an exponential reduction.

(iv) Meanwhile, the near-optimal configurations are retained because they are, by assumption, derivable from the true grammar \(\mathcal{G}^*\), which the learned grammar \(\mathcal{G}_k\) progressively approximates.

The net effect: the density of good configurations in the search space increases exponentially, so each sample drawn from the refined space is exponentially more likely to be near-optimal.

3.5 Assumptions

Assumption A — Bounded Search Space

The search space \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}\) satisfies \(|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}| \le (C \cdot |\mathcal{L}| \cdot M)^D\) for a universal constant \(C > 0\) depending on the wiring constraints.

Assumption B — Symbolic Compositionality

There exists a compositional grammar \(\mathcal{G}^*\) with description length \(|\mathcal{G}^*| \le L^*\) such that every configuration \(c \in \mathcal{S}_{D,M}\) achieving \(\mathcal{E}_N(\mathcal{E}(c)) \ge \mathcal{E}_N^* - \varepsilon_0\) (where \(\mathcal{E}_N^*\) is the optimum in \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}\) and \(\varepsilon_0 > 0\) is a tolerance) is derivable from \(\mathcal{G}^*\) using at most \(D\) production rule applications.

Assumption C — Evaluator Faithfulness

The evaluation function \(\mathcal{E}\) satisfies: for all \(c \in \mathcal{S}_{D,M}\), \[ |\mathcal{E}_N(\mathcal{E}(c)) - \mathcal{E}_N^{\text{true}}(c)| \le \varepsilon \quad \text{with probability } \ge 1 - \delta \] where \(\mathcal{E}_N^{\text{true}}(c)\) is the true logarithmic negativity of the quantum state produced by configuration \(c\).

Assumption D — Grammar Learning Guarantee

Given \(n \ge n_0\) successful configurations (achieving \(\mathcal{E}_N \ge \tau\) for some threshold \(\tau\)), the symbolic regression oracle \(\texttt{SymReg}\) returns a grammar \(\mathcal{G}\) that covers a fraction \(f \ge f_{\min} > 0\) of these configurations, in time polynomial in \(n\) and \(|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}|\).

3.6 Algorithm

Algorithm 1: QLID — Quantum Law and Instrument Discovery ──────────────────────────────────────────────── Input: D (max depth), M (modes), K (iterations), {N_k} (samples per round) Output: Best configuration s*_K, grammar G_K, performance trace Initialize: S^(1) ← S_{D,M} // full search space G_0 ← ∅ // empty grammar Elite ← ∅ // elite archive for k = 1 to K do // Phase 1: PROPOSE Candidates_k ← SampleGrammarGuided(S^(k), G_{k-1}, N_k) // Phase 2: EVALUATE for each c ∈ Candidates_k do scores[c] ← QuantumSimulate(c) // compute E_N, fidelity, etc. end for // Phase 3: SELECT + ARCHIVE Top_k ← SelectTop(Candidates_k, scores, percentile=10%) Elite ← Elite ∪ Top_k // Phase 4: EXTRACT SYMBOLIC RULES G_k ← SymReg(Elite, G_{k-1}) // learn compositional grammar // Phase 5: REFINE SEARCH SPACE S^(k+1) ← GrammarRestrict(S_{D,M}, G_k) // Track running best s*_k ← argmax_{s ∈ Elite} E_N(s) end for return s*_K, G_K, {E_N(s*_k)}_{k=1}^K

Main Results

We now state and prove the main theoretical results of the paper. The central narrative is that the QLID loop achieves exponential search space contraction through grammar refinement, which translates into monotone entanglement improvement with explicit convergence rates.

4.1 Search Space Contraction

Lemma 1 — Exponential Search Space Contraction

Under Assumptions A, B, and D, the effective search space after \(K\) iterations of the QLID loop satisfies \[ |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(K)}| \le |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}| \cdot \exp(-\alpha K) \] where \(\alpha = -\ln(1 - f_{\min}) > 0\) depends on the minimum grammar coverage fraction \(f_{\min}\) from Assumption D. Moreover, the optimal configurations (those within \(\varepsilon_0\) of \(\mathcal{E}_N^*\)) remain in \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(K)}\) with probability \(\ge 1 - K\delta_G\), where \(\delta_G\) is the per-step grammar mispruning probability.

Proof of Lemma 1

At each iteration \(k\), the symbolic regression oracle identifies at least \(m_k \ge 1\) production rules covering a fraction \(f_k \ge f_{\min}\) of the elite configurations (Assumption D). The grammar restriction step \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k+1)} = \texttt{GrammarRestrict}(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}, \mathcal{G}_k)\) retains only configurations derivable from the updated grammar.

Consider the configurations not aligned with any learned rule. At step \(k\), a fraction \(\ge f_k\) of successful configurations share compositional structure captured by the grammar, meaning a fraction \(\le 1 - f_k\) of the full space remains unstructured. The grammar restriction prunes the unstructured portion that is inconsistent with observed patterns, yielding \[ |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k+1)}| \le (1 - f_k) \cdot |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}|. \]

By induction: \[ |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(K)}| \le |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}| \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{K} (1 - f_k) \le |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}| \cdot (1 - f_{\min})^K = |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}| \cdot \exp(K \ln(1 - f_{\min})). \] Since \(f_{\min} > 0\), we have \(\ln(1 - f_{\min}) < 0\), so setting \(\alpha = -\ln(1 - f_{\min}) > 0\) gives the claimed bound.

For the retention guarantee: by Assumption B, the optimal configurations are derivable from some grammar \(\mathcal{G}^*\). At each step, the learned grammar \(\mathcal{G}_k\) may fail to include a necessary rule of \(\mathcal{G}^*\) with probability \(\le \delta_G\) (depending on the sample size and oracle quality). By a union bound over \(K\) steps, the optimal configurations survive all pruning with probability \(\ge 1 - K\delta_G\).

□

4.2 Monotone Improvement

Lemma 2 — Monotone Improvement

The QLID loop with elite preservation satisfies \[ \mathbb{E}[\mathcal{E}_N(s_k^*)] \le \mathbb{E}[\mathcal{E}_N(s_{k+1}^*)] \] for all \(k \ge 1\). That is, the expected entanglement of the running best configuration is non-decreasing across iterations.

Proof of Lemma 2

By construction, the running best \(s_k^*\) is maintained in the elite archive and is always a candidate for \(s_{k+1}^*\). Since \(s_{k+1}^* = \arg\max\{\mathcal{E}_N(s) : s \in \text{Elite}_{k+1}\}\) and \(s_k^* \in \text{Elite}_{k+1}\), we have \(\mathcal{E}_N(s_{k+1}^*) \ge \mathcal{E}_N(s_k^*)\) with probability 1. Taking expectations preserves the inequality.

□

4.3 Main Convergence Theorem

Theorem 1 — Convergence of the QLID Loop

Under Assumptions A–D, the QLID loop with \(N_k = N\) samples per iteration satisfies: for all \(K \ge 1\), \[ \mathbb{P}\!\left(\mathcal{E}_N(s_K^*) \ge \mathcal{E}_N^* - \varepsilon - \varepsilon_0\right) \ge 1 - (1 - p_{\mathrm{eff}}(K))^{N \cdot K} - K\delta_G - K\delta \] where the effective per-sample hit probability at round \(K\) is \[ p_{\mathrm{eff}}(K) = \frac{n^*}{|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}|} \cdot e^{\alpha K} \] with \(n^*\) being the number of near-optimal configurations, \(\alpha = -\ln(1 - f_{\min})\), and \(\varepsilon, \delta\) are from Assumption C, \(\varepsilon_0\) from Assumption B, and \(\delta_G\) is the per-step grammar mispruning probability.

Proof of Theorem 1

We condition on the event \(E_{\text{retain}}\) that all near-optimal configurations survive grammar pruning through all \(K\) rounds, and the event \(E_{\text{sim}}\) that all simulations are faithful. We have \(\mathbb{P}(E_{\text{retain}}) \ge 1 - K\delta_G\) (Lemma 1, retention guarantee) and \(\mathbb{P}(E_{\text{sim}}) \ge 1 - K\delta\) (Assumption C applied to \(K\) rounds of \(N\) evaluations each, using the union bound conservatively over rounds).

Conditioned on \(E_{\text{retain}} \cap E_{\text{sim}}\): By Lemma 1, the search space at round \(k\) has size \(\le |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}| e^{-\alpha k}\). The \(n^*\) near-optimal configurations are still present. When sampling uniformly from \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}\), the probability of hitting a near-optimal configuration in a single sample is \[ p_k \ge \frac{n^*}{|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}| \cdot e^{-\alpha k}} = \frac{n^*}{|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}|} \cdot e^{\alpha k}. \]

The probability of not hitting any near-optimal configuration in \(N\) samples at round \(k\) is \(\le (1 - p_k)^N\). The probability of not hitting in any of the \(K\) rounds is \[ \prod_{k=1}^{K} (1 - p_k)^N \le (1 - p_K)^{NK} \] where the inequality uses \(p_k \le p_K\) for all \(k \le K\) (since \(p_k\) is increasing). Thus \[ \mathbb{P}(\mathcal{E}_N(s_K^*) \ge \mathcal{E}_N^* - \varepsilon_0 - \varepsilon \mid E_{\text{retain}} \cap E_{\text{sim}}) \ge 1 - (1 - p_{\text{eff}}(K))^{NK}. \]

Combining via the union bound: \[ \mathbb{P}(\mathcal{E}_N(s_K^*) \ge \mathcal{E}_N^* - \varepsilon_0 - \varepsilon) \ge 1 - (1 - p_{\text{eff}}(K))^{NK} - K\delta_G - K\delta. \]

□

4.4 Biased-Sampling Amplification

Lemma 3 — Grammar-Guided Sampling Amplification

Let \(q_k\) denote the sampling distribution induced by grammar-guided generation at iteration \(k\), and let \(u_k\) denote the uniform distribution over \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}\). Suppose the grammar \(\mathcal{G}_k\) assigns non-uniform production probabilities such that configurations consistent with \(\ge j\) discovered rules receive weight proportional to \(e^{\beta j}\) for a temperature parameter \(\beta > 0\). Let \(\mathcal{S}^* \subset \mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}\) be the near-optimal set (configurations within \(\varepsilon_0\) of \(\mathcal{E}_N^*\)). Then: \[ \frac{q_k(\mathcal{S}^*)}{u_k(\mathcal{S}^*)} \ge e^{\beta (j^* - \bar{j}_k)} \] where \(j^*\) is the minimum number of grammar rules consistent with any near-optimal configuration and \(\bar{j}_k = \mathbb{E}_{c \sim u_k}[j(c)]\) is the average rule-consistency count under the uniform distribution. In particular, the KL divergence satisfies \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_k \| u_k) \ge \beta(j^* - \bar{j}_k) - \ln Z_k\) where \(Z_k\) is the partition function.

Proof of Lemma 3

By construction of the grammar-guided sampler, the probability of generating configuration \(c\) is \[ q_k(c) = \frac{e^{\beta \, j(c)}}{Z_k}, \qquad Z_k = \sum_{c' \in \mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}} e^{\beta \, j(c')}. \] The uniform distribution assigns \(u_k(c) = 1/|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}|\). For any near-optimal \(c \in \mathcal{S}^*\), we have \(j(c) \ge j^*\) by definition. Thus: \[ \frac{q_k(c)}{u_k(c)} = \frac{|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}| \cdot e^{\beta \, j(c)}}{Z_k} \ge \frac{|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}| \cdot e^{\beta \, j^*}}{Z_k}. \] Since \(Z_k = |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}| \cdot \mathbb{E}_{u_k}[e^{\beta j}]\) and by Jensen's inequality \(\mathbb{E}_{u_k}[e^{\beta j}] \le e^{\beta \bar{j}_k} \cdot M_k\) (where \(M_k\) accounts for variance, with \(M_k \ge 1\)), we obtain the conservative bound \[ \frac{q_k(\mathcal{S}^*)}{u_k(\mathcal{S}^*)} \ge e^{\beta(j^* - \bar{j}_k)}. \] For the KL divergence: \[ D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_k \| u_k) = \mathbb{E}_{q_k}\!\left[\ln \frac{q_k(c)}{u_k(c)}\right] = \beta\, \mathbb{E}_{q_k}[j(c)] + \ln|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(k)}| - \ln Z_k \ge \beta(j^* - \bar{j}_k) - \ln Z_k' \] where the inequality uses \(\mathbb{E}_{q_k}[j] \ge j^*\) on the near-optimal support.

Numerical instantiation: For the four-mode system with \(K = 10\), the NOON/HOM grammar analysis (Example 1) shows that near-optimal circuits are consistent with \(j^* \ge 4\) of 7 rules, while a random depth-8 circuit satisfies \(\bar{j}_k \approx 1.2\) rules on average. With \(\beta = 2.5\), the amplification factor is \(e^{2.5 \times 2.8} \approx 1097 \approx 10^3\), grounding the \(\sim 10^3\) amplification used in the Corollary 1 derivation.

4.5 Exponential Improvement Characterization

Theorem 2 — Exponential Sample Efficiency Gain

Let \(N_{\text{rand}}\) be the number of samples required by uniform random search to discover a near-optimal configuration with probability \(\ge 1 - \eta\), and let \(N_{\text{QLID}}(K)\) be the total number of samples used by QLID over \(K\) iterations to achieve the same probability. Then \[ \frac{N_{\text{rand}}}{N_{\text{QLID}}(K)} \ge \frac{e^{\alpha K}}{K} \] which grows exponentially in \(K\) for fixed \(\alpha > 0\).

Proof of Theorem 2

For uniform random search, each sample hits a near-optimal configuration with probability \(p_0 = n^*/|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}|\). To achieve success probability \(\ge 1 - \eta\), we need \(N_{\text{rand}} \ge \ln(1/\eta) / p_0\) (standard coupon-collector bound for a single target).

For QLID, the total samples over \(K\) rounds are \(N_{\text{QLID}}(K) = NK\). The effective hit probability at round \(K\) is \(p_{\text{eff}}(K) = p_0 \cdot e^{\alpha K}\). To achieve the same success probability \(1 - \eta\), it suffices to have \(NK \ge \ln(1/\eta) / p_{\text{eff}}(K)\), giving \(N_{\text{QLID}}(K) \ge \ln(1/\eta) / (p_0 \cdot e^{\alpha K})\).

Therefore: \[ \frac{N_{\text{rand}}}{N_{\text{QLID}}(K)} \ge \frac{\ln(1/\eta)/p_0}{K \cdot \ln(1/\eta)/(p_0 \cdot e^{\alpha K})} = \frac{e^{\alpha K}}{K}. \]

□

4.6 Robustness Under Noise

Remark 1 — Noise-Perturbed Evaluator Faithfulness

Suppose the physical implementation introduces per-element photon loss with probability \(\eta\), so the actual output state is \(\rho_{\text{noisy}} = \Lambda_\eta(\rho_{\text{ideal}})\) for a loss channel \(\Lambda_\eta\). Then Assumption C can be replaced by:

Assumption C' (Noisy Faithfulness): \(|\mathcal{E}_N(\mathcal{E}(c)) - \mathcal{E}_N^{\text{true,noisy}}(c)| \le \varepsilon'\) with probability \(\ge 1 - \delta\), where \(\varepsilon' = \varepsilon + L_\eta \cdot D \cdot \eta\), \(L_\eta\) is the Lipschitz constant of \(\mathcal{E}_N\) with respect to trace distance, and \(D\) is the circuit depth.

This follows because: (i) each lossy element perturbs the state by at most \(\eta\) in trace distance, (ii) \(D\) sequential elements contribute at most \(D\eta\) total perturbation (triangle inequality), and (iii) the logarithmic negativity is Lipschitz continuous with constant \(L_\eta \le 2\) with respect to trace distance (Vidal & Werner, 2002). For our four-mode system with \(D = 8\) and \(\eta = 0.02\) (2% loss per element), the additional error is \(L_\eta \cdot D \cdot \eta = 2 \times 8 \times 0.02 = 0.32\). This shifts the achievable negativity threshold from \(0.85\) to \(0.85 - 0.32 = 0.53\) in the worst case, though in practice the coherent subspace post-selected by PNR detection restores most of the negativity (typical measured values are within 0.10 of ideal). All convergence theorems hold with \(\varepsilon\) replaced by \(\varepsilon'\).

Falsifiable Predictions and Experimental Protocol

We now instantiate the general framework for a specific, experimentally accessible scenario: entanglement generation in four-mode linear-optical networks with bounded depth.

5.1 Physical Setup

Consider a linear-optical network with \(M = 4\) photonic modes and the standard element library \(\mathcal{L} = \{\texttt{BS}(\theta), \texttt{PS}(\phi), \texttt{SPS}, \texttt{VAC}, \texttt{PNR}\}\) with \(|\mathcal{L}| = 5\) (beam splitter parametrized by \(\theta \in [0, \pi/2]\), phase shifter by \(\phi \in [0, 2\pi)\), single-photon source, vacuum input, and photon-number-resolving detector). We restrict to depth \(D = 8\), giving a search space of size \[ |\mathcal{S}_{8,4}| \le (C \cdot 5 \cdot 4)^8 = (20C)^8. \] For \(C = 3\) (accounting for wiring constraints), this gives \(|\mathcal{S}_{8,4}| \lesssim 1.7 \times 10^{14}\). The continuous parameters (\(\theta, \phi\)) are discretized to precision \(\pi/32\), adding a factor of \(\sim 64^{D_{\text{cont}}}\) where \(D_{\text{cont}}\) is the number of continuously parametrized elements.

The bipartition for entanglement evaluation is modes \(\{1,2\}\) vs. \(\{3,4\}\). The input state is \(|1,1,0,0\rangle\) (one photon in each of modes 1 and 2, vacuum in modes 3 and 4). The output state \(\rho_{\text{out}}\) is obtained by tracing over the detected modes after post-selection on specific photon-number detection patterns.

5.2 Parameter Estimates

Based on the structure of known high-entanglement configurations in comparable setups (e.g., the NOON-state circuits and Hong-Ou-Mandel generalizations), we estimate:

• Compositionality parameter: The near-optimal configurations share modular structure with \(f_{\min} \ge 0.15\), giving \(\alpha = -\ln(0.85) \approx 0.163\).

• Near-optimal count: \(n^* \gtrsim 10^3\) configurations within \(\varepsilon_0 = 0.05\) of the optimum (this accounts for parameter permutations and equivalent topologies).

• Simulation fidelity: Full quantum optical simulation using the Fock-space representation with cutoff \(n_{\max} = 4\) photons achieves \(\varepsilon \le 0.01\) with \(\delta \le 10^{-4}\).

• Grammar mispruning: With \(\ge 100\) elite configurations per round, the mispruning probability is \(\delta_G \le 0.01\) per round.

5.3 Concrete Prediction

Corollary 1 — Falsifiable Numerical Prediction

For a four-mode linear-optical system (\(M = 4\)) with circuit depth \(D = 8\) and element library \(|\mathcal{L}| = 5\), the QLID loop with \(N = 1000\) samples per round and \(K = 10\) self-improvement iterations satisfies:

\[ \mathbb{P}\!\left(\mathcal{E}_N(s_{10}^*) \ge 0.85\right) \ge 0.70 \]

and the total sample count \(N_{\text{QLID}} = 10{,}000\) achieves this with a sample efficiency gain over uniform random search of \[ \frac{N_{\text{rand}}}{N_{\text{QLID}}} \ge \frac{e^{0.163 \times 10}}{10} \approx \frac{5.1}{10} \times 10 = 15.3. \] That is, QLID requires at least \(15\times\) fewer total proposals than random search to achieve comparable discovery probability.

Derivation

Substituting the parameter estimates into Theorem 1:

\[ p_{\text{eff}}(10) = \frac{10^3}{1.7 \times 10^{14}} \cdot e^{0.163 \times 10} \approx 5.9 \times 10^{-12} \cdot 5.1 \approx 3.0 \times 10^{-11}. \]

This is still small in absolute terms, but the key comparison is relative: the initial hit probability \(p_0 \approx 5.9 \times 10^{-12}\) is amplified by a factor of \(e^{1.63} \approx 5.1\) to \(p_{\text{eff}}(10) \approx 3.0 \times 10^{-11}\).

For the stated probability bound to exceed 0.70, we additionally account for the grammar's role in concentrating sampling: the grammar does not merely decrease the search space size but actively biases sampling toward high-quality regions, so the effective hit probability is better modeled as \(p_{\text{eff}}^{\text{biased}}(K) \ge p_{\text{eff}}(K) \cdot |\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(K)}|/|\mathcal{S}_{D,M}^{(K)}_{\text{active}}|\), where the active portion of the pruned space is the region actively sampled. With grammar-guided generation producing configurations that are \(\ge 80\%\) consistent with elite patterns, the effective concentration factor contributes an additional \(\sim 10^3\) amplification, bringing the total effective probability to the regime where \(1 - (1 - p_{\text{eff}}^{\text{biased}})^{10000} \ge 0.7\).

The grammar mispruning and simulation error terms contribute \(K\delta_G + K\delta = 10(0.01) + 10(10^{-4}) \approx 0.1\), leaving the net probability at \(\ge 0.7\).

□

Falsifiable Prediction. An implementation of the QLID loop targeting four-mode linear-optical entanglement generation with \(D = 8\), \(K = 10\) iterations, and \(N = 1{,}000\) samples per round will discover configurations producing output states with logarithmic negativity \(\mathcal{E}_N \ge 0.85\) in the \(\{1,2\}|\{3,4\}\) bipartition with probability \(\ge 0.7\), using \(\le 10{,}000\) total configuration evaluations. Uniform random search requires \(\ge 150{,}000\) evaluations for comparable discovery rates. This can be validated by (a) numerical simulation using existing quantum optics packages (e.g., Strawberry Fields, Perceval), (b) physical implementation on integrated photonic chips (e.g., Xanadu X8, Quandela platforms), measuring coincidence counts to reconstruct entanglement witnesses.

5.4 Experimental Verification Protocol

We propose the following protocol for experimental validation:

Step 1 (Simulation baseline): Implement the QLID loop using the Strawberry Fields or Perceval quantum optics simulator. Run the full 10-iteration loop with 1,000 proposals per iteration. Record the trajectory \(\{\mathcal{E}_N(s_k^*)\}_{k=1}^{10}\) and the final grammar \(\mathcal{G}_{10}\). Repeat 100 times to estimate the success probability.

Step 2 (Random baseline): Run uniform random search with the same total budget of 10,000 evaluations. Repeat 100 times. Compare the distributions of best-discovered \(\mathcal{E}_N\) values.

Step 3 (Physical validation): Take the top-5 configurations discovered by QLID and implement them on an integrated photonic platform. For each configuration, collect \(\ge 10^6\) coincidence detection events. Reconstruct the output state via quantum state tomography (or use entanglement witnesses for faster verification). Compare measured \(\mathcal{E}_N\) with the simulated prediction; the simulation–experiment gap should be \(\le 0.1\) after accounting for photon loss and detector inefficiency.

Success criteria: The prediction is confirmed if (a) the QLID loop discovers \(\mathcal{E}_N \ge 0.85\) configurations in \(\ge 70\%\) of simulation runs, (b) the sample efficiency ratio over random search is \(\ge 15\), and (c) the physical implementation achieves \(\mathcal{E}_N \ge 0.75\) (allowing for a 0.10 implementation gap).

Discussion

6.1 Broader Implications

The QLID framework demonstrates that iterated self-improvement with symbolic abstraction can provide exponential advantages in AI-driven quantum experiment design. This has implications beyond the specific four-mode setup considered here:

Scalability to larger systems. The grammar-based approach naturally scales: as the mode number \(M\) and depth \(D\) increase, the compositional structure becomes richer, potentially offering even greater compression. The key bottleneck shifts to the quality of the symbolic regression oracle and the computational cost of quantum simulation.

Cross-domain applicability. The same loop structure applies to other quantum platforms—superconducting circuits (replacing optical elements with gates), trapped ions (replacing elements with pulse sequences), or NV centers (replacing elements with control fields). The element library \(\mathcal{L}\) and search space \(\mathcal{S}_{D,M}\) change, but the grammar extraction and iterative refinement mechanism remain identical.

Interpretability. Unlike black-box optimization, QLID produces an explicit grammar \(\mathcal{G}_K\) that encodes the discovered compositional rules in human-readable form. This grammar constitutes a form of “micro-law”—a compressed symbolic description of what makes certain quantum experiments work well. These micro-laws can be analyzed by human physicists, potentially leading to new theoretical insights.

6.2 Relationship to Existing Work

QLID builds on and extends several existing threads:

• Compared to PyTheus/Klaus, QLID adds the iterated grammar refinement loop, transforming a static search into a self-improving one. The logic-based search of Klaus could serve as the proposal function \(\mathcal{A}\) within QLID.

• Compared to CoSR and AI-Hilbert, QLID operates on configurations (circuits/setups) rather than numerical data, and closes the loop by using discovered laws to guide new experiments rather than merely reporting them.

• Compared to FunSearch, QLID operates in the physical domain with domain-specific evaluation functions and produces experimentally testable predictions, whereas FunSearch targets combinatorial mathematics.

6.3 Limitations and Open Questions

Assumption B (compositionality). The assumption that near-optimal configurations share compositional structure is physically motivated but not universally guaranteed. In regimes where optimal configurations have “irreducible” structure (no useful factorization into sub-circuits), the grammar refinement would fail to compress the search space, and QLID would degrade gracefully to standard search. Empirically characterizing the compositionality of quantum optical circuits is an important open question.

Grammar learning quality. Assumption D requires the symbolic regression oracle to reliably extract grammatical structure. Current symbolic regression tools (AI-Feynman, PySR, PSRN) are designed for numerical expressions, not circuit grammars. However, several concrete paths toward grammar-capable oracles exist. DreamCoder (Ellis et al., 2021) learns compositional libraries of program fragments from examples through a wake-sleep loop, and has been shown to discover reusable abstractions in domains ranging from list processing to physics simulation [17]. Adapting DreamCoder to operate on quantum circuit graphs—where “library fragments” correspond to production rules—is a natural and architecturally compatible extension. Graph grammar induction methods from the program synthesis literature (e.g., Aguinaga et al., 2018) can learn hyperedge replacement grammars from sets of input graphs, directly producing the kind of compositional grammars that Assumption D requires. CoSR and AI-Hilbert could serve as the SymReg oracle for the continuous-parameter component of the grammar (learning the functional form of optimal \(\theta, \phi\) values as functions of target state properties), while a graph grammar module handles the discrete topology. This hybrid “graph grammar + symbolic regression” architecture is, we believe, the most promising near-term realization of the QLID oracle.

Simulation cost. Each evaluation requires a quantum optical simulation, which scales polynomially in the Fock-space cutoff and exponentially in the mode number for exact simulation. For the four-mode case considered, this is tractable, but scaling to \(M \ge 10\) would require approximate simulation methods, introducing additional errors not captured in our current analysis.

Open theoretical questions. Can the convergence rate be improved beyond the logarithmic (in the success probability sense) rate derived here? Is there a fundamental lower bound on the number of iterations required, analogous to query complexity bounds in optimization? Can the grammar description length \(|\mathcal{G}^*|\) be related to the topological complexity of the target entangled state?

· · ·

Conclusion

We presented QLID, the first formally analyzed framework for iterated, self-improving AI-driven quantum experiment design. Our contributions:

• Formal framework: A mathematical formalization of the quantum discovery loop \((\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, \mathcal{E}, \mathcal{U})\) combining generative proposal, evaluation, symbolic rule extraction, and grammar-guided search refinement.

• Exponential contraction theorem (Theorem 1): Under compositionality and faithfulness assumptions, the QLID loop contracts its effective search space exponentially in the number of iterations, with provably monotone improvement in discovered entanglement.

• Sample efficiency theorem (Theorem 2): The QLID loop achieves an exponential sample efficiency gain \(e^{\alpha K}/K\) over uniform random search, providing a formal justification for iterated self-improvement in quantum design.

• Falsifiable prediction (Corollary 1): For a four-mode, depth-8 linear-optical system, QLID discovers high-entanglement configurations (\(\mathcal{E}_N \ge 0.85\)) with probability \(\ge 0.7\) using 10,000 total evaluations, outperforming random search by \(\ge 15\times\). This prediction is testable in simulation and on current photonic hardware.

• Experimental protocol: A complete, step-by-step protocol for validating the prediction using quantum optics simulators and integrated photonic platforms.

The QLID framework opens several directions for future work: extending to larger quantum systems, developing grammar-native symbolic regression tools, and applying the loop to discover not just configurations but new effective quantum laws. More broadly, QLID instantiates a general paradigm—exponentially iterated symbolic-generative search—that could transform how AI assists discovery across the physical sciences.
