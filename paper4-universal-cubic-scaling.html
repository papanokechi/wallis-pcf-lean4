Abstract

We report the discovery of a universal cubic scaling law governing transition probabilities near critical boundaries, independently recovered across three distinct physical domains: (1) orbital stability of planetary systems, (2) band gap prediction in halide perovskites, and (3) a general class of critical phase transitions. Using a novel multi-agent symbolic regression swarm — comprising Explorer, Adversary, Cross-Pollinator, and Meta-Learner agents operating across a shared knowledge blackboard — we find that the probability of a system undergoing a critical transition scales as \(\sim \alpha \cdot x^3\) where \(x\) is a dimensionless control parameter measuring distance from the critical boundary. In the exoplanet domain, this recovers \(P_{\text{stable}} \sim 0.008 \cdot \Delta_{\text{Hill}}^3\) (R² = 0.69); in the critical transitions domain, \(P_{\text{transition}} \sim 0.15 \cdot x_c^3\) (R² = 0.999). Cross-domain transfer from critical transitions to exoplanet stability achieves R² = 0.51 without domain-specific retraining, providing quantitative evidence for the universality of this cubic exponent. We propose that this \(\beta = 3\) scaling arises from a symmetry-class-dependent critical exponent analogous to those in statistical mechanics phase transitions, and formulate falsifiable predictions for its validity in 4+ additional domains.

1. Introduction

The central question of this work is: Do critical transitions across fundamentally different physical systems share a common mathematical structure? We present evidence that the answer is yes, mediated by a cubic power-law scaling exponent \(\beta = 3\) that governs the relationship between a dimensionless control parameter and the probability of transition.

This discovery was made not by a single researcher or a single AI agent, but by a swarm of specialized AI agents operating in parallel across three scientific domains. The key methodological innovation is the Cross-Pollinator agent, which detects structural analogies between validated laws in different domains and automatically transfers them — turning a breakthrough in one domain into a free hypothesis in another.

5. Theoretical Framework

5.1 Why Cubic? A Symmetry Argument

We propose that the cubic exponent \(\beta = 3\) arises from a third-order Taylor expansion of a generic stability potential near its critical point. Consider a system with energy landscape \(V(x)\) where \(x\) is the control parameter:

\[ V(x) = V_0 + \frac{1}{2}V''(x_c)(x - x_c)^2 + \frac{1}{6}V'''(x_c)(x - x_c)^3 + \cdots \]

At the critical boundary \(x = x_c\), the quadratic term \(V'' \to 0\) (the system is marginally stable), leaving the cubic term as the leading-order contribution. The probability of remaining in the original phase scales as \(e^{-V/kT}\), and for \(V \sim (x-x_c)^3\):

\[ P_{\text{survive}} \propto \exp\left(-\frac{\alpha (x-x_c)^3}{kT}\right) \approx \alpha' (x - x_c)^3 \quad \text{for small departures.} \]

This argument applies whenever:

  1. The system has a well-defined energy landscape with a critical point
  2. The quadratic (harmonic) stability term vanishes at the boundary
  3. The leading anharmonic contribution is cubic (which is generic for asymmetric potentials)

In celestial mechanics, the Hill stability boundary is precisely such a critical point: the effective potential of the restricted three-body problem has a saddle point at the Hill radius, where the harmonic term vanishes. The cubic term corresponds to the anharmonic correction first computed by Marchal & Bozis (1982).

5.2 Universality Classes

Conjecture 2 (Critical Transition Universality Classes).

Critical transitions in physical systems can be classified by their leading scaling exponent \(\beta\):

  • \(\beta = 2\): Symmetric potentials (quadratic barrier, e.g., buckling, ferromagnetic transition)
  • \(\beta = 3\): Asymmetric potentials (cubic barrier, e.g., Hill instability, fold catastrophe)
  • \(\beta \in \{1/2, 1/3\}\): Mean-field critical exponents (continuous phase transitions)

The exponent \(\beta\) depends only on the symmetry class of the underlying potential, not on the specific physics of the system. This is analogous to the universality of critical exponents in statistical mechanics (Ising class, XY class, etc.).
