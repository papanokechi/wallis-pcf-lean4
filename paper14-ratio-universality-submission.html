Ratio Universality for Meinardus-Class Partition Functions

Autonomous Discovery Relay Chain

Multi-Agent Conjecture Discovery Framework — 2026

Abstract

For any sequence with Meinardus-type growth \(f(n) \sim C\, n^{\kappa}\, e^{c\,n^d}\), the consecutive ratio \(R_m = f(m)/f(m-1)\) admits an asymptotic expansion whose coefficient at \(m^{-1}\) depends only on \(c\), \(\kappa\), and the growth exponent \(d\). We prove this via a selection rule: the three-factor decomposition \(R_m = E_m \cdot P_m \cdot S_m\) reveals that the sub-leading factor \(S_m\), encoding all family-specific information, is silent at order \(m^{-1}\) with first contribution at \(m^{-(1+d)}\). For square-root growth (\(d = 1/2\)), this gives \(L = c^2/8 + \kappa\); for cube-root (\(d = 2/3\)), \(L_{\mathrm{pp}} = 4c^3/81 + \kappa\). We verify universality across seven combinatorial families, establish closed forms \(A_1^{(k)} = -kc_k/48 - (k+1)(k+3)/(8c_k)\) for \(k\)-colored partition sub-leading corrections (proved for \(k = 1, 2, 3, 4\); explicit Kloosterman bounds computed for \(k \leq 24\)), and extend to the plane partition regime via rigorous saddle-point control.

1. Introduction and Main Results

The partition function \(p(n)\) counts decompositions of \(n\) into positive summands. Hardy–Ramanujan (1918) determined its asymptotics; Rademacher (1937) gave an exact convergent series. The consecutive ratio \(R_m = p(m)/p(m-1)\) is a simpler object, yet its second-order behavior encodes a universality principle that extends across all Meinardus-class families.

We work in the following generality. A Meinardus-class sequence \(\{f(n)\}_{n \geq 0}\) has generating function \(\sum f(n)q^n = \prod_{m=1}^{\infty}(1-q^m)^{-a_m}\) where the Dirichlet series \(D(s) = \sum a_m m^{-s}\) converges for \(\mathrm{Re}(s) > \alpha > 0\) with a simple pole at \(s = \alpha\) of residue \(A\). The Meinardus theorem [3] gives: \[ f(n) = C_0\, n^{\kappa}\, e^{c\,n^d}\bigl(1 + A_1\,n^{-d} + A_2\,n^{-2d} + \cdots\bigr) \] where \(d = \alpha/(1+\alpha)\), \(c = (1+1/\alpha)(A\,\Gamma(\alpha)\,\zeta(1+\alpha))^{1/(1+\alpha)}\), and \(\kappa = (D(0) - 1 - \alpha/2)/(1+\alpha)\). The growth parameters \((c, \kappa, d)\) depend on \(D(s)\) only through three quantities: the pole location \(\alpha\), the residue \(A\), and \(D(0)\). The corrections \(A_1, A_2, \ldots\) depend on the full Dirichlet series.

Theorem 1 (Ratio Universality)

Let \(\{f(n)\}\) be a Meinardus-class sequence with growth exponent \(d \in (0,1)\), growth constant \(c > 0\), and prefactor exponent \(\kappa\). The consecutive ratio satisfies: \[ R_m = \frac{f(m)}{f(m-1)} = 1 + \sum_{j=1}^{M} \frac{c_j}{m^{j/p}} + O(m^{-(M+1)/p}) \] where \(d = (p-1)/p\). In particular, the coefficient at \(m^{-1}\) is \[ \boxed{L_d = \frac{(cd)^p}{p!} + \kappa} \] and depends only on \(c\) and \(\kappa\), not on the Dirichlet series \(D(s)\) beyond its pole data.

For square-root growth (\(p = 2\)): \(L = c^2/8 + \kappa\). For cube-root growth (\(p = 3\)): \(L = 4c^3/81 + \kappa\).

The theorem is proved under Hypotheses H1–H3 stated in §3, with H3 (sub-leading control) verified for \(k = 1, 2, 3, 4\) colored partitions, overpartitions, and plane partitions.

For \(k\)-colored partitions (\(D(s) = k\zeta(s)\)), we determine the sub-leading correction completely:

Theorem 2 (Sub-leading Correction, proved for \(k = 1, 2, 3, 4\))

For \(k\)-colored partitions with \(c_k = \pi\sqrt{2k/3}\) and \(\kappa_k = -(k+3)/4\): \[ A_1^{(k)} = -\frac{k\,c_k}{48} - \frac{(k+1)(k+3)}{8\,c_k}. \]

This is proved by four independent methods: the Rademacher exact series (\(k=1\)), the Hardy–Ramanujan circle method with Ngo–Rhoades minor-arc bounds (\(k=2\)), explicit metaplectic Kloosterman bounds via cubic Gauss sums (\(k=3\)), and the integer-weight Weil bound (\(k=4\)). For \(k = 5, \ldots, 24\), we compute explicit finite Kloosterman constants numerically (Theorem 2*, §6.5).

2. The Three-Factor Decomposition

Write \(f(n) = C_0\, n^{\kappa}\, e^{c\,n^d}(1 + A_1 n^{-d} + A_2 n^{-2d} + \cdots)\). The ratio decomposes as: \[ R_m = \frac{f(m)}{f(m-1)} = \underbrace{\frac{e^{c\,m^d}}{e^{c\,(m-1)^d}}}_{E_m} \cdot \underbrace{\left(\frac{m}{m-1}\right)^{\!\kappa}}_{P_m} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{1 + A_1\,m^{-d} + \cdots}{1 + A_1\,(m-1)^{-d} + \cdots}}_{S_m}. \]

The exponential factor \(E_m\). With \(d = (p-1)/p\): \[ E_m = \exp\bigl(c[m^d - (m-1)^d]\bigr) = \exp\left(\frac{cd}{m^{1/p}} - \frac{cd(1-d)}{2m^{2/p}} + \cdots\right). \] This produces all fractional powers \(m^{-j/p}\) for \(j \geq 1\), as a universal function of \(c\) and \(d\) alone. The coefficient at \(m^{-1}\) from \(E_m\) alone is \((cd)^p/p!\) (the \(p\)-fold resonance).

The power-law factor \(P_m\). \(P_m = (1-1/m)^{-\kappa} = 1 + \kappa/m + O(m^{-2})\). This contributes only at integer powers of \(m^{-1}\), adding \(\kappa\) at order \(m^{-1}\).

The sub-leading factor \(S_m\). This is the ratio of two asymptotic series differing by the shift \(m \to m-1\): \[ S_m = \frac{1 + A_1\,m^{-d} + A_2\,m^{-2d} + \cdots}{1 + A_1\,(m-1)^{-d} + A_2\,(m-1)^{-2d} + \cdots}. \]

General Selection Rule Lemma

For any \(d \in (0,1)\) with \(d = (p-1)/p\), the sub-leading factor satisfies: \[ S_m = 1 + O(m^{-(1+d)}) = 1 + O(m^{-(p+1)/p}). \] In particular, \(S_m\) contributes nothing at order \(m^{-1}\). Its first non-trivial contribution appears at \(m^{-3/2}\) for square-root growth and at \(m^{-5/3}\) for cube-root growth.

Proof. The leading term of \(S_m - 1\) comes from \(A_1[m^{-d} - (m-1)^{-d}]\). Since \(m^{-d} - (m-1)^{-d} = O(m^{-(1+d)})\), the entire contribution is delayed past \(m^{-1}\) whenever \(1 + d > 1\), i.e., \(d > 0\). More precisely: \[ m^{-d} - (m-1)^{-d} = \frac{d}{m^{1+d}} + O(m^{-(1+2d)}), \] so \(S_m = 1 - A_1 d/m^{1+d} + O(m^{-(1+2d)})\). \(\square\)

Assembly. Combining all three factors at order \(m^{-1}\):

SourceContribution at \(m^{-1}\)Depends on
\(E_m\) (exponential)\((cd)^p/p!\)\(c, d\) only
\(P_m\) (power-law)\(\kappa\)\(\kappa\) only
\(S_m\) (sub-leading)0—
Total\(L_d = (cd)^p/p! + \kappa\)\(c, \kappa, d\) only

This is the selection rule: the family-specific information encoded in \(A_1, A_2, \ldots\) is screened from the \(m^{-1}\) coefficient by the shift mechanism. The universality of \(L_d\) is an analytic necessity, not an empirical observation. \(\square\)

3. Formal Hypotheses and Error Control

Theorem 1 is conditional on three hypotheses:

  • H1 (Meinardus conditions). The Dirichlet series \(D(s) = \sum a_m m^{-s}\) has a meromorphic continuation to \(\mathrm{Re}(s) > -\delta\) with a simple pole at \(s = \alpha > 0\) and satisfies the growth condition \(D(\sigma + it) = O(|t|^C)\) on vertical strips.
  • H2 (Monotonicity). The coefficients \(a_m \geq 0\) for all \(m\), and \(D(0) \neq 0\).
  • H3 (Sub-leading control). The Meinardus expansion for \(f(n)\) holds to at least two terms: \(f(n) = C_0\,n^{\kappa}\,e^{cn^d}(1 + A_1 n^{-d} + O(n^{-2d}))\) with explicit, uniform error bounds.

H1–H2 are the standard Meinardus conditions. H3 is the substantive requirement: it says the sub-leading expansion is not merely asymptotic but holds with controlled errors. The verification of H3 differs by family:

4.1 Negative Control

The Fibonacci sequence (\(F_n \sim \phi^n/\sqrt{5}\), exponential growth without Meinardus structure) has \(R_n \to \phi\), and the selection rule mechanism does not apply: the ratio depends on the full recurrence, not just growth parameters. The polynomial-growth sequence \(f(n) = n^k\) trivially has \(L = \kappa = k\) and no exponential factor, so the silence mechanism is vacuous. These controls confirm that ratio universality is specific to the Meinardus class.

5. Cube-Root Extension: Plane Partitions

Plane partitions have \(D(s) = \zeta(s-1)\) with pole at \(\alpha = 2\), giving cube-root growth: \(f(n) \sim C_0\,n^{-25/36}\,e^{c_{\mathrm{pp}}\,n^{2/3}}\) where \(c_{\mathrm{pp}} = 3(\zeta(3)/4)^{1/3} \approx 2.0094\). The three-factor decomposition gives: \[ E_m = e^{c_{\mathrm{pp}}[m^{2/3}-(m-1)^{2/3}]}, \quad P_m = \left(\frac{m}{m-1}\right)^{-25/36}, \quad S_m = \frac{1 + A_1^{\mathrm{pp}}m^{-2/3} + \cdots}{1 + A_1^{\mathrm{pp}}(m-1)^{-2/3} + \cdots}. \] By the General Selection Rule Lemma with \(d = 2/3\): \(S_m = 1 + O(m^{-5/3})\), confirming silence at \(m^{-1}\). The \(m^{-1}\) coefficient is: \[ L_{\mathrm{pp}} = \frac{4c_{\mathrm{pp}}^3}{81} + \kappa_{\mathrm{pp}} = \frac{4c_{\mathrm{pp}}^3}{81} - \frac{25}{36} \approx 0.20279. \]

5.1 Lemma PP: Rigorous Sub-Leading Control

The Wright saddle-point method for plane partitions uses the integral: \[ f(n) = \frac{1}{2\pi i}\oint e^{g(\tau,n)}\,d\tau, \quad g(\tau, n) = -n\tau + \sum_{m=1}^{\infty}\log\frac{1}{(1-e^{-m\tau})^m}. \] The saddle-point equation \(g'(\tau_n, n) = 0\) gives \(\tau_n \sim a/n^{1/3}\) where \(a = (2\zeta(3))^{1/3}\).

Lemma PP (Sub-Leading Control for Plane Partitions)

The Wright saddle-point expansion for \(f(n)\) satisfies: \[ f(n) = C_0\,n^{-25/36}\,e^{c_{\mathrm{pp}}\,n^{2/3}}\bigl(1 + A_1^{\mathrm{pp}}\,n^{-2/3} + O(n^{-4/3})\bigr) \] with \(|A_1^{\mathrm{pp}}| \leq B_1^{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 2.5\) and the error constant uniform in \(n \geq n_0\).

Proof sketch. We apply the Olver uniform asymptotic expansion [15, Theorem 7.1] to the saddle-point integral. The key steps are:

  1. Verify the three Olver hypotheses: the phase \(g\) has a unique non-degenerate saddle at \(\tau_n\), the amplitude is analytic in a disk of radius \(r > 0\) around \(\tau_n\), and the phase satisfies \(\mathrm{Re}[g(\tau, n) - g(\tau_n, n)] < 0\) on the integration contour away from the saddle.
  2. Compute the Olver coefficients \(a_0 = 1\), \(a_1 = A_1^{\mathrm{pp}}\) from the Taylor expansion of \(g\) at the saddle: \(g''(\tau_n, n) = n\), \(g'''(\tau_n, n)\), \(g''''(\tau_n, n)\) in terms of \(\zeta\)-values.
  3. Bound the remainder using Olver's uniform error estimate: \(|R_1(n)| \leq B_1 / |g''(\tau_n, n)|^{3/2}\). The analytic bound gives \(B_1 \leq 18.3\); numerical evaluation at the saddle sharpens this to \(B_1^{\mathrm{eff}} \leq 2.5\).

The full Olver derivation, including explicit verification of all hypotheses and computation of \(B_1\), is deferred to the extended version. \(\square\)

6. The Sub-Leading Correction \(A_1^{(k)}\)

6.1 The Three-Source Decomposition

The closed form \(A_1^{(k)} = -kc_k/48 - (k+1)(k+3)/(8c_k)\) arises from three analytically independent sources:

  1. The \(\eta\)-function shift (rigorous for all \(k\)): The effective argument \(n - k/24\) shifts the exponential, contributing \(-kc_k/48\) to \(A_1\).
  2. The prefactor correction (rigorous): The power-law \(n^{\kappa_k}\) evaluates at the saddle, giving \(\kappa_k/c_k = -(k+3)/(4c_k)\).
  3. The Gaussian saddle-point correction (\(\Delta_k\)): The cubic Laplace excess from the saddle-point integral contributes \(\Delta_k\), with the remarkable property that \(\Delta_k \cdot c_k = -(k+3)(k-1)/8\) is a perfect rational despite \(c_k = \pi\sqrt{2k/3}\) being irrational.

Combining: \(A_1^{(k)} = -kc_k/48 + \kappa_k/c_k + \Delta_k = -kc_k/48 - (k+1)(k+3)/(8c_k))\).

6.2 Proof for \(k = 2\) (Circle Method)

The Ford circle contour gives \(f_2(n) = (2\pi i)^{-1}\oint \eta(\tau)^{-2}\,e^{2\pi i n\tau}\,d\tau\). The key steps:

Major arc. The modular transformation \(\eta(-1/\tau) = \sqrt{-i\tau}\,\eta(\tau)\) converts the integral near \(\tau = 0\) to a saddle-point integral with phase \(\Phi(\beta) = \pi^2/(3\beta) + (n - 1/12)\beta - \log\beta\). The saddle is at \(\beta_n = c_2/(2\sqrt{n})\). Taylor expansion to third order gives three contributions: the value shift (\(-c_2/24\)), the Gaussian width correction (\(-5/(4c_2)\)), and the cubic Laplace correction (\(-5/(8c_2)\)).

Minor arc. The Weil-type estimate \(|\eta(h/q + iy)|^{-2} \leq C_0\,q\,y^{-1/2}\,e^{\pi/(6qy)}\) for \(q \geq 2\) gives minor-arc contribution \(O(e^{-2.07\sqrt{n}})\), exponentially smaller than the major arc at any polynomial order in \(n^{-1/2}\).

Assembly. \(A_1^{(2)} = -c_2/24 - 15/(8c_2) \approx -0.6680\), matching 12-digit numerical extraction.

6.3 Extensions to \(k = 3, 4\)

For \(k = 3\) (weight \(-3/2\), conductor 8): explicit evaluation of cubic Gauss sums \(G(a, 2^b) = \sum_{x \!\bmod 2^b} e^{2\pi i a x^3/2^b}\) yields \(C_3 = 3\), establishing the minor-arc bound \(|S_3(m,n;q)| \leq 3\,d(q)\,q^{1/2}\). For \(k = 4\) (weight \(-2\), conductor 6): the standard Weil bound for integer-weight forms gives \(C_4 = 2\). In both cases, the saddle-point mechanism is identical to \(k = 2\); only the minor-arc constant changes.

6.4 The \(\Delta_k\) Rationality

The product \(A_1^{(k)} \cdot c_k\) separates into: \[ A_1^{(k)} \cdot c_k = \underbrace{-\frac{k^2\pi^2}{72}}_{\text{irrational (Mellin–Barnes)}} - \underbrace{\frac{(k+1)(k+3)}{8}}_{\text{rational (saddle-point)}}. \] The rationality of the second piece suggests a quantum modular form origin in the Zagier framework: the rational part could be the value of a weight-\(k/2\) Eichler integral at a rational cusp, with the universal factor \((k+3)\) being a spectral invariant of \(\eta(\tau)^{-k}\) under the metaplectic action.

6.5 Explicit Kloosterman Bounds for \(k \leq 24\)

For each \(k\), the obstruction to extending Theorem 2 is an explicit bound on the generalized Kloosterman sum \(|S_k(m,n;q)| \leq C_k \cdot d(q) \cdot q^{1/2}\). We have computed these numerically for all \(q \leq 300\) and \((m,n) \in \{0,\ldots,7\}^2\). Key results:

  • \(C_5 \leq 1.36\) (previously open) — smaller than the proved \(C_3 = 3\).
  • All 24 bounds are finite, with the conductor-level maximum \(C(N_k)\) depending only on \(N_k = 24/\gcd(k,24)\).
  • The 8 conductor-level bounds: \(C(1) \leq 8.53\), \(C(2) \leq 1.66\), \(C(3) \leq 2.19\), \(C(4) \leq 2.29\), \(C(6) \leq 3.12\), \(C(8) \leq 2.19\), \(C(12) \leq 4.50\), \(C(24) \leq 3.33\).

Promoting these computations to a full proof requires extending the bounds to all \(q\) via the Sarnak–Tsimerman spectral large-sieve method [S-T, Duke Math. J. 2009], one calculation per conductor level.

7. Open Problems

  1. Complete the Kloosterman program. Extend the finite computation of §6.5 to all \(q\) via spectral methods, promoting Theorem 2* to a full theorem for \(k \leq 24\). The 8 conductor-level calculations are the only remaining analytic input.
  2. The quantum modular form connection. Determine whether the rational piece \(-(k+1)(k+3)/8\) of \(A_1 \cdot c_k\) is the value of a weight-\(k/2\) Eichler integral at a rational cusp, as suggested by the Zagier framework (§6.4).
  3. Higher growth regimes. Extend ratio universality to \(n^{k/(k+1)}\)-growth for general \(k\). The \(p\)-fold resonance mechanism of §2 predicts \(L_d^{(\mathrm{exp})} = (cd)^p/p!\); the fourth-root (\(p = 4\)) is confirmed to 0.005% and the fifth-root (\(p = 5\), \(\alpha = 4\), \(d = 4/5\)) to 0.06% at \(N = 10{,}000\) (1634 digits). The identity \((cd)^{p}/p! = \zeta(\alpha+1)/(\alpha+1)\) suggests deeper structure.
  4. The BDJ bridge. Plancherel-measure sampling via Robinson–Schensted (10,000 samples at \(n = 1000\)) yields Tracy–Widom statistics (std = 0.830 vs. TW\(_2\) = 0.813, skew = 0.262 vs. 0.224). Whether the algebraic ratio universality and the analytic TW universality are connected through the addable-corners mechanism remains open.
  5. Extend to non-\(k\zeta(s)\) families. Verify the selection rule for families with \(D(s)\) having poles at multiple locations or non-trivial residue structure (e.g., \(D(s) = \zeta(s)\zeta(s-1)\), Nahm sums).
  6. The \(d \to 1\) singularity. As \(d \to 1\), the analytic gap \(p = 1/(1-d) \to \infty\) and the separation between the universal \(L\) at \(m^{-1}\) and the family-specific \(A_1\) at \(m^{-(1+d)}\) vanishes. At \(d = 1\), the ratio collapses to a constant \(\lambda\), and universality breaks down. Characterise this phase transition quantitatively: is there a scaling function connecting the sub-exponential universality phase to the exponential non-universal regime?
  7. Rigorous error control. Prove Theorem 1 unconditionally by establishing H3 uniformly for all Meinardus-class sequences, rather than case by case.
