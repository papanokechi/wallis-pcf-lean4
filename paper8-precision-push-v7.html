The γ/ν = 2.035 (3.6% error, R² = 0.998) is the most accurate FSS result, because \(\chi_{\max}(L)\) does not require the exact \(T_c\) — it simply finds the peak. This is the standard approach used in the literature [1, 2].

The β/ν = 0.779 is biased high because M(T_c) is evaluated at the discovered T_c (2% too high), placing us slightly in the disordered phase where M drops faster with L. This is a well-known sensitivity: even 1% Tc error systematically biases β/ν. The narrow-range direct fit (§6) compensates for this.

14. What We Learn from the Failures

14.1 AIC prefers logarithmic for M(t)

This tells us the full data range includes crossover effects. The power law is the correct asymptotic form, but at L = 12 with Tc 2% off, the data spans both scaling and non-scaling regimes. Solution: use the narrow range (which independently gives β = 0.329), or use larger lattices at more temperatures near Tc.

14.2 β/ν from FSS is biased (50% error)

M(Tc, L) is extremely sensitive to Tc accuracy. Even 2% Tc error places us slightly in the disordered phase, where M drops faster with L. In contrast, χ_max(L) is insensitive to Tc because it's a peak property. The lesson: for M-based exponents, Tc accuracy at the 0.1% level is necessary before FSS works well.

14.3 γ from all single-L methods is 15–23% off

The susceptibility exponent γ is harder to pin down from single-L fits because χ data is noisier and the scaling regime is narrower. The FSS ratio γ/ν = 2.035 (3.6%) is much more reliable because it uses the information from all five lattice sizes simultaneously.
