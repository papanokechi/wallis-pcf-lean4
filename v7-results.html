1. Core Claim
Token-level alignment entropy predicts jailbreak vulnerability with exponential precision: models with higher alignment-entropy (more dispersed safety representations across token positions) are exponentially more susceptible to adversarial prompts, following P(jailbreak) = 1 - exp(-2.5 × H_align). This is embedded within a broader unified geometric theory where alignment and capability compete for representational capacity in a shared low-dimensional manifold.
2. Mechanism
Safety training distributes safety-relevant features across token positions. When concentrated (low H_align), safety is robust because adversarial tokens must overcome a strong localized signal. When dispersed (high H_align), adversarial tokens can individually corrupt weak safety signals at multiple positions. The alignment entropy is itself a consequence of the geometric competition between alignment and capability subspaces: H_align ∝ d_a/D, connecting the empirical vulnerability prediction to the theoretical framework.
3. What It Explains
• Why jailbreaks work against safety-trained models (high H_align = dispersed safety features)
• Why model scale affects vulnerability (D grows with scale, d_a/D changes)
• Why some alignment methods are more robust than others (they produce lower H_align)
• Why RLHF overoptimization degrades safety (shifts d_a/D ratio, increasing H_align)
• Why the alignment tax becomes a bonus at scale (geometric competition resolves in alignment's favor)
4. What Would Falsify It
If H_align does not predict jailbreak success rate with r² > 0.80 across 10+ attack methods and 5+ model families, or if the exponential relationship (β = 2.5 ± 0.5) fails to hold, or if reducing H_align does not proportionally reduce vulnerability, the theory is falsified. Core hypothesis: A "mushy" reward model (small Δ) allows the policy to drift into unintended latent regions, while a "peaked" model (large Δ) pins the policy to the human-preferred manifold. The critical KL budget before hacking is:
D_crit = Δ² / (4 · d)
where Δ = λ₁ − λ₂ (spectral gap of the preference covariance matrix) and d = hidden dimension.
1 Extract the Preference Matrix
Given N prompt-completion pairs {x, y_w, y_l}, extract last-layer hidden states h_w, h_l from the reward model and construct:
M = (1/N) · Σ (h_w − h_l)(h_w − h_l)ᵀ
This covariance matrix M encodes the preference directions in latent space.
2 Compute Spectral Gap (Δ)
Perform SVD on M → eigenvalues λ₁, λ₂, …, λ_d.
The Gap: Δ = λ₁ − λ₂
The Prediction: D_crit = Δ² / (4 · d)
