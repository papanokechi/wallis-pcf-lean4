Abstract

We describe an autonomous, self-iterating multi-agent system for Ramanujan-style mathematical discovery. The pipeline generates, validates, and stress-tests conjectures across six families: generalised 1/π series, continued fractions, q-series identities, mock theta functions, partition congruences, and the Ramanujan tau function. A blackboard architecture coordinates seven specialised agents (two explorers, one validator, one adversary, one refiner, one cross-pollinator, one meta-learner) over five self-iterating rounds.

Methodological contribution. We introduce an honest claim taxonomy that strictly separates (i) rediscoveries of known results from (ii) genuinely novel conjectures. Every discovery is assigned one of six statuses: candidate, verified_numeric, verified_known, novel_unproven, novel_proven, or falsified. Content-hash deduplication eliminates the common failure mode of counting the same identity five times across parameter variations. Partition congruences are automatically checked against Ramanujan's three classical congruences and their corollaries before any novelty claim is made.

Results. In 191 seconds the v3 system produces 43 unique discoveries: 13 verified rediscoveries of known results (6 partition corollaries, 5 Lehmer verification steps, 2 continued-fraction identities correctly caught by ISC-style constant recognition), and 29 falsified candidates correctly rejected. Zero results are falsely labelled “proven” or falsely labelled “novel.” The v3 ISC-style transform lookup, expanded literature database (25+ known results), and CF fixed-point proof engine eliminate the false-novelty claims present in v2.

Introduction

Srinivasa Ramanujan's mathematical legacy includes thousands of identities — many stated without proof, many discovered by intuitive pattern recognition rather than formal derivation. This makes Ramanujan's work a natural testbed for automated mathematical discovery: can a machine, searching systematically over parameter spaces and formula families, rediscover classical identities and perhaps find new ones?

Several systems have attempted this. The Ramanujan Machine project[1] discovered conjectural continued-fraction representations for fundamental constants using distributed search. Borwein and Bailey's experimental mathematics programme[2] used PSLQ integer-relation algorithms to identify previously unknown series for π. Partition congruence search dates to Ramanujan's original three (mod 5, 7, 11) and was massively extended by Ono[3], Ahlgren and Boylan[4], and computational surveys by Radu[5].

A persistent methodological problem in automated discovery systems is honest claim taxonomy. Systems routinely label known results as “proven” or “discovered,” count the same identity multiple times across parameter variations, and present error = ∞ alongside confidence = 1.0. These reporting failures are not merely cosmetic: they make it impossible for a reader to assess which outputs, if any, constitute genuine contributions.

This paper addresses these failures directly. We describe a system (Section 2) that searches six formula families via seven agents; a claim taxonomy (Section 3) that classifies every discovery honestly; our experimental results (Section 4); a detailed analysis of novel candidates (Section 5); and an honest assessment of limitations (Section 6).

2.3 Agent Ensemble

Seven agents coordinate via a thread-safe blackboard:

  • Explorer × 2 (seed=42, seed=137): Generate conjectures, budget = 20 per strategy per round.
  • Validator: Three-tier numeric precision (50/200/500 digits), SymPy symbolic verification, PSLQ cross-check, convergence analysis.
  • Adversary: Parameter sensitivity (±10%), extreme-precision retest, edge cases, triviality detection, PSLQ stability audit.
  • Refiner: Fine-grid search (Δa ∈ [−3, 3], Δb ∈ [−10, 10]) around validated results.
  • Cross-pollinator: Structural similarity scoring across family pairs.
  • Meta-learner: Discovery-rate trend analysis and strategy recommendation.

2.4 Content-Hash Deduplication

A critical v2 innovation is content-hash deduplication. Each discovery has a canonical_key derived from its family and normalised parameters — not its random ID. For example, every partition congruence is keyed on part_{a}_{b}_{m}, so p(5n+4) ≡ 0 (mod 5) can never be counted twice regardless of how many rounds or agents rediscover it. This eliminates the common failure of counting the same theorem 5× across generations.

Honest Claim Taxonomy

The most important design choice in this system is the six-state status lifecycle, which prevents the agent from ever labelling a rediscovery as “proven by agent.”

Status lifecycle:
candidate → verified_numeric → {verified_known | novel_unproven} → {novel_proven | falsified}

3.2 Corollary Detection

For partition congruences, a dedicated corollary checker tests whether p(an+b) ≡ 0 (mod m) follows from one of Ramanujan's three classical congruences (m = 5: p(5n+4); m = 7: p(7n+5); m = 11: p(11n+6)). If a is a multiple of the base modulus and b mod a equals the base residue, the congruence is automatically classified as verified_known with a citation. For example, p(10n+4) ≡ 0 (mod 5) is flagged as a corollary of p(5n+4) ≡ 0 (mod 5) because 10 = 2 × 5 and 4 mod 5 = 4.

3.3 Error-Infinity Bug Fix

A subtle but critical bug in v1: discoveries could carry error = ∞ while simultaneously having confidence = 1.0. This contradiction arose because partition congruences have exact (integer) validation but the initialiser defaulted error to ∞. In v2, the add_verification() method always replaces ∞ with the measured error, and the _post() method in the agent replaces ∞ with None before writing to the blackboard.

The Ono-framework moduli (13, 17, 19, …) did not yield new congruences within the search range — an expected result consistent with the sparsity theorems of Ahlgren and Boylan[4].

Continued fractions (v3): In v2, two polynomial CFs (the negative golden ratio and 1 + √5) were flagged as novel_unproven because the literature database lacked classical CF identities. In v3, the ISC-style constant recognition (14 target constants × rational multipliers |p|, |q| ≤ 4, plus algebraic transforms CF2, 1/CF, CF2−1, CF+1/CF) immediately catches these as transforms of φ. Both are now verified_known.

Nonpolynomial CFs (v3 — new): The nonpolynomial coefficient search (factorial, Fibonacci, exponential, prime-mixed) produced zero matches against the 14-constant target list within tolerance 10−8. This is not surprising: CFs with rapidly-growing coefficient sequences (e.g., a(n) = n!) tend to either diverge or converge to transcendental values not in the classical constant catalogue. These represent genuinely unexplored territory; the challenge is developing recognition criteria for their limits.

Novel Candidate Analysis

In v2, two continued-fraction identities survived all validation without matching known results. In v3, both are correctly classified as verified_known thanks to the expanded literature database and ISC-style transform lookup. We discuss the reclassification and its implications.

5.1 Reclassified: CF-1 (a(n) = [1], b(n) = [−1]) → −φ

v2 status: novel_unproven
v3 status: verified_known
Value: −1.618033988749895… (= −φ)
v3 detection: ISC-style lookup matches −1.0 × CF = φ at error 2.19 × 10−56
Citation: Negative branch of golden ratio CF; classical result

The v3 ISC-style lookup checks the GCF value against 14 target constants with rational multipliers p/q for |p|, |q| ≤ 4. The match −1 × CF = φ is found immediately. In v2, the literature database only stored explicit key lookups, missing this trivial transform.

5.2 Reclassified: CF-2 (a(n) = [4], b(n) = [2]) → 1 + √5

v2 status: novel_unproven
v3 status: verified_known
Value: 3.23606797749979… (= 1 + √5 = 2φ + 1)
v3 detection: ISC-style lookup matches −0.5 × CF = φ at error 2.19 × 10−56
Proof (v3 engine): Fixed-point: y = 2 + 4/y ⇒ y2 − 2y − 4 = 0 ⇒ y = 1 + √5  ✓

This GCF converges to 1 + √5 = 2φ + 1. The v3 CF fixed-point proof engine automatically derives: for constant a(n) = 4, b(n) = 2, the self-similar structure gives y = 2 + 4/y, hence y2 − 2y − 4 = 0. Discriminant Δ = 4 + 16 = 20. Positive root: y = (2 + √20)/2 = 1 + √5. The ISC lookup simultaneously catches this as −0.5 × CF = φ, confirming it as a known constant via a different route.

5.3 v3 Novelty Assessment

With the expanded literature database (25+ known results covering partition congruences, classical CF identities, and Swinnerton-Dyer tau congruences) and the ISC-style transform lookup (14 constants × rational multipliers × algebraic transforms), v3 produces zero false-novelty claims. This is the correct outcome: the search space explored by the current system (polynomial CFs with small coefficients, simple partition congruences, tau function residues) has been thoroughly catalogued by human mathematicians.

Key finding. The primary improvement from v2 to v3 is not the discovery of new results, but the elimination of false-novelty claims. This is the more important methodological contribution: a system that honestly reports “0 novel” when searching well-explored territory is more trustworthy than one that claims “2 novel” by missing classical references.

Discussion

6.1 What the System Does Well

  • Honest taxonomy works. Zero results are falsely labelled proven or novel. All 13 known results carry citations. The error-infinity bug is eliminated.
  • ISC-style constant recognition (v3). The transform lookup (14 constants × rational multipliers × algebraic transforms) catches all elementary CF identities that v2 missed.
  • CF fixed-point proof engine (v3). For constant-coefficient GCFs, the system automatically derives the quadratic fixed-point equation and verifies symbolically via SymPy.
  • Deduplication works. Content-hash keying prevents double-counting of the same identity across rounds and agents.
  • Adversarial rejection works. The 67.4% falsification rate demonstrates active rejection of numerical coincidences.
  • Partition corollary detection works. All six partition congruences correctly identified as corollaries of classical results.
  • Reproducibility (v3). Every run produces a structured reproducibility bundle (agent version, library versions, config, timestamp) in results/reproducibility.json.

6.2 What the System Does Not Do

  • No genuinely novel identities. With the expanded literature database, all results are correctly classified as known. The system does not yet produce genuinely new mathematics — but now it honestly reports this fact rather than inflating novelty claims.
  • Nonpolynomial CFs produce no matches. The factorial, Fibonacci, exponential, and prime-mixed CF searches (v3) converge to values outside the 14-constant target catalogue. These are genuinely unexplored but require new recognition criteria beyond ISC-style lookup.
  • Partition search scope. Despite extending to Ono moduli (13, 17, 19, 23, 29, 31, 37, 41, 43), no new congruences were found in the search range a ≤ 100, n ≤ 1000. This is consistent with Ahlgren–Boylan[4].
  • No pi series or q-series discoveries. The Ramanujan–Chudnovsky sweep and q-series hunting produced zero viable candidates. The parameter spaces for known 1/π formulas are extremely sparse.

6.3 Structural Barriers to Novelty

Our results illustrate a fundamental tension in automated mathematical discovery: the most searchable formula families (polynomial CFs, linear partition congruences) are also the most thoroughly explored by human mathematicians. Genuinely novel identities are likely to emerge from:

  • Higher-dimensional parameter spaces (multivariate q-series, mock modular forms)
  • Non-polynomial continued fractions with transcendental coefficient sequences (v3 introduced factorial/Fibonacci/exponential CFs; the next step is systematic enumeration of their limits)
  • Partition congruences at composite moduli requiring modular-form-theoretic proofs
  • Connections between formula families (the cross-pollinator agent found no bridges in this run)
  • Expanding the target constant catalogue beyond 14 constants to include algebraic number detection (minimal polynomial search via LLL/PSLQ)

6.4 Reproducibility

Every discovery carries a provenance record: method, parameters, precision, random seed (explorers use seed=42 and seed=137), and generation number. v3 adds a structured reproducibility bundle saved to results/reproducibility.json containing: agent version (3.0.0), UTC timestamp, Python version, platform, library versions (mpmath, sympy), and the full run configuration. The run is deterministic given the same seeds and can be reproduced with:

python -m ramanujan_agent --rounds 5 --budget 20

For the results reported in this paper: Python 3.14.3, mpmath 1.3.0, sympy 1.14.0, Windows 11, 191.4 s total on commodity hardware.

Related Work

Ramanujan Machine[1]. Raayoni et al. (2021) used distributed BOINC search over polynomial continued fractions to discover conjectural representations for π, e, and the Catalan constant. Their system does not distinguish rediscoveries from novel results.

Experimental mathematics[2]. Bailey, Borwein, and Plouffe used PSLQ to discover the BBP formula for π. Our PSLQ strategy follows their approach but adds multi-precision stability checking (doubled precision, same relation required) to filter numerical coincidences.

Partition congruences[3,4,5]. Ono (2000) proved that for every prime m ≥ 5, infinitely many partition congruences exist modulo m. Ahlgren and Boylan (2003) showed that Ramanujan's three are the only “simple” congruences for m ∈ {5, 7, 11}. Our search confirmed this computationally: no new simple congruences were found for any modulus, and all discovered congruences were corollaries.

AI for mathematics. Recent systems include AlphaGeometry[7] (Trinh et al., 2024) for Olympiad geometry and FunSearch[8] (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024) for extremal combinatorics. Unlike these systems, our approach uses no neural networks — it is purely algorithmic search with multi-agent coordination.

Conclusion

We have presented a self-iterating multi-agent system for Ramanujan-style mathematical discovery that prioritises honest reporting over inflated novelty claims. The v3 system introduces ISC-style constant recognition, nonpolynomial CF search strategies, a CF fixed-point proof engine, and a structured reproducibility bundle.

In 191 seconds the system produced 43 unique discoveries. All 13 known results are correctly labelled with citations — including the two CFs that v2 had misclassified as novel. All 29 false candidates are correctly falsified. Zero results carry false novelty claims. The main contribution remains methodological: a strict claim taxonomy, ISC-style transform detection, content-hash deduplication, automated corollary detection, and multi-precision stability checking that together ensure every output is classified honestly.

The honest result “0 novel discoveries” is itself informative: it quantifies the difficulty of finding genuinely new mathematics via systematic search of well-explored formula families. Future work should focus on expanding the target constant catalogue (algebraic number detection via LLL/PSLQ), systematic enumeration of nonpolynomial CF limits, and neural-guided search to explore higher-dimensional parameter spaces where novel identities may reside.
