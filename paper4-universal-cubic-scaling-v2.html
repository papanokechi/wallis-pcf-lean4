Abstract

We report the discovery — and rigorous examination — of a cubic scaling law governing transition probabilities near critical boundaries, found by a multi-agent symbolic regression swarm operating across three domains. In the critical transitions domain, the law \(y \approx 0.150 \cdot x^3\) achieves R² = 0.999 and survives Benjamini-Hochberg correction at \(\alpha = 0.05\). In the exoplanet stability domain, a cubic fit \(P \sim 0.008 \cdot \Delta_{\text{Hill}}^3\) achieves R² = 0.66 but [Rev] mechanistic analysis shows the quadratic term does not vanish (|a₂/a₃| = 5.8), placing exoplanets in a mixed scaling regime. The materials domain exhibits a distinct exponent class (β ≈ −2/3). Cross-domain transfer succeeds in one direction (critical→exoplanet, R² = 0.49) but fails in 14/18 attempts, with [Rev] root-cause analysis attributing 56% of failures to domain-support mismatch, 22% to variable mismatch, and 6% to numerical instability. [Rev] Agent ablation shows near-zero marginal contribution from the Adversary and Pollinator agents in the current implementation, identifying a clear optimization target. We temper the original universality claim: cubic scaling is a plausible hypothesis for asymmetric potentials where the harmonic term vanishes, but present evidence only confirms it in one (synthetic) domain. We outline a replication plan to test the conjecture on real datasets.

1. Introduction

The central question of this work is: Do critical transitions across different physical systems share a common mathematical structure? Our initial answer was an enthusiastic yes, based on cubic exponents appearing in 2/3 studied domains. This revised manuscript provides a more nuanced assessment.

We use a swarm of specialized AI agents operating in parallel: Explorers run symbolic regression independently per domain, Adversaries validate the top candidates, a Cross-Pollinator transfers laws across domains, and a Meta-Learner tracks process-level patterns. [Rev] We now include ablation experiments to quantify each agent type's marginal contribution.

The three study domains are:

  1. Exoplanet orbital stability: whether 2-planet systems remain Hill-stable for \(10^5\) orbits (N = 300, up from 200 in v1)
  2. Perovskite band gap: Eg of ABX₃ halide perovskites (N = 200 augmented, up from 40 in v1 — [Rev] Fix 1)
  3. Universal critical transitions: synthetic systems with known cubic ground truth (N = 300)

The critical transitions dataset has a known analytic ground truth, giving it an inherent advantage. 2.3 Statistical Framework [Rev Fix 3]

Each discovered law is tested for statistical significance using an F-test against the null model (R² = 0), yielding a p-value. We then apply Benjamini-Hochberg false discovery rate correction at \(\alpha = 0.05\) across all 30 discovered laws simultaneously to control the expected proportion of false positives.

Definition (BH Procedure).

Given \(m\) hypothesis tests with ordered p-values \(p_{(1)} \leq \cdots \leq p_{(m)}\), reject \(H_{(i)}\) for all \(i \leq k\), where \(k = \max\{i: p_{(i)} \leq i \cdot \alpha / m\}\).

Key Finding: Exoplanet Cubic Claim Not Supported Mechanistically [Rev]

In the exoplanet domain, the quadratic coefficient is 5.8× larger than the cubic coefficient. A pure quadratic fit (R² = 0.706) outperforms a pure cubic fit (R² = 0.664). This means the condition \(V''(x_c) \to 0\) — required for the theoretical cubic-dominance argument — does not hold for the Hill stability problem as modeled here. The cubic term contributes, but the quadratic term dominates.

This is an honest negative result. The exoplanet stability boundary is not well described by an asymmetric anharmonic potential with vanishing harmonic term; rather, it exhibits a mixed quadratic-cubic landscape.

Transfer Failure Diagnosis [Rev Fix 6]

Of 18 transfer attempts:

  • Domain support mismatch (10/18, 56%): The source expression produces values on a scale completely different from the target. For example, critical→materials maps cubic-growing law onto bounded band-gap values.
  • Variable mismatch (4/18, 22%): No source-expression variables have entries in the mapping table (exoplanet→materials direction).
  • Numerical instability (1/18, 6%): Expression produces NaN/Inf due to negative bases with fractional exponents.
  • Success (3/18, 17%): Only critical→exoplanet (R² = 0.49) and materials→critical after refit (R² = 0.80, 0.26).

The extremely negative R² values (e.g., −2180) are not numerical errors but reflect a legitimate scale mismatch: the transferred expression predicts values 100–1000× the target range. A better transfer protocol would include automatic z-score normalization before evaluation.

The ablation experiment reveals that the Adversary and Cross-Pollinator agents provide near-zero marginal value in the current implementation. Root causes:

  1. Adversary: All 15 validated laws (of 15 reviewed) would have been accepted anyway — the symbolic regression engine already produces high-quality fits, and the 0.6/0.3 thresholds are too lenient.
  2. Pollinator: Only 1/18 transfers succeeded in the full pipeline and 0/8 in the ablation variant. The variable mappings are too crude for productive transfer.
  3. Score metric: The aggregate score is dominated by mean R² (which is identical across ablations since the same SR engine produces the same laws). A better metric would weight novelty, diversity, and unexpected discoveries.

Implication: The multi-agent framework is currently a pipeline, not a true swarm. Agents do not yet influence each other's behavior, create feedback loops, or improve across rounds. This is the single highest-priority area for improvement.

4. The Cubic Scaling Conjecture — Tempered

Conjecture (Weakened)

For systems with an asymmetric stability potential where the harmonic term vanishes at the critical boundary (\(V''(x_c) = 0\)), the order parameter scales as

\[ y(x) = \alpha \cdot |x - x_c|^3 + \mathcal{O}(|x-x_c|^4) \]

Evidence status: Confirmed in 1/3 domains (synthetic critical transitions). Inconclusive in exoplanets (quadratic dominates). Refuted for materials (different exponent class).

We originally claimed the cubic exponent was "universal." The mechanistic analysis (§3.3) forces us to restrict this claim:

  1. Critical transitions: The |a₂/a₃| ratio is 0.08, confirming the quadratic term vanishes and the cubic term dominates. This is the strongest evidence — but the dataset is synthetic with a known cubic ground truth, so recovery is expected, not surprising.
  2. Exoplanets: |a₂/a₃| = 5.8 — the quadratic term does not vanish. The Hill stability boundary is not a pure asymmetric saddle; it has significant quadratic curvature. A cubic law still fits reasonably (R² = 0.66) but is not the best description.
  3. Materials: The best exponent is −2/3, not −3. Band gap scaling with ionic radius follows fundamentally different physics (tight-binding theory predicts rational exponents from orbital overlap integrals).

4.1 Theoretical Argument (Scope Clarified)

The symmetry argument for cubic scaling remains valid within its premises:

\[ V(x) = V_0 + \frac{1}{2}\underbrace{V''(x_c)}_{\to 0}(x-x_c)^2 + \frac{1}{6}V'''(x_c)(x-x_c)^3 \]

When \(V'' \to 0\), cubic dominates. [Rev] The key question is which systems actually satisfy \(V'' = 0\)? Our evidence shows:

  • Fold catastrophes (cusp bifurcations) satisfy it by definition — this is the Arnol'd classification A₃.
  • The Hill stability boundary does not satisfy it perfectly — the effective restricted three-body potential has a saddle with non-zero Hessian eigenvalues.
  • Materials band gap has no critical-boundary structure at all for the rX dependence — it is a smooth scaling relationship.

5. What Actually Worked: Critical → Exoplanet Transfer

Despite the many transfer failures, one result stands out: the law \(0.150 \cdot x^3\) from critical transitions, mapped to \(0.150 \cdot \Delta_{\text{Hill}}^3\), achieves R² = 0.49 on the exoplanet test set without any domain-specific training.

This is significant for two reasons:

  1. A cubic expression discovered on one dataset generalizes to another at R² ≈ 0.5, suggesting structural similarity even if the exoplanet potential is not purely cubic.
  2. After coefficient refit, materials→critical transfer also achieves R² = 0.80, demonstrating that cross-domain transfer can work when the functional form is compatible and scales are appropriately normalized.

The lesson: transfer success requires (a) functional form compatibility, (b) scale alignment, and (c) feature semantics matching. When all three hold, R² > 0.4. When any one fails, R² < −1 is common.

6. Replication Plan

[Rev] To move this conjecture from "plausible hypothesis" to "confirmed result," we outline a pre-registered replication plan:

6.1 Independent Datasets

  1. Castelli perovskites: Use the matminer database (~18,000 DFT-computed band gaps) to test whether \(r_X^{-2/3}\) holds with real data and whether any cubic term emerges.
  2. Kepler multi-planet systems: Use the Kepler DR25 catalog (~700 multi-planet systems) with SPOCK stability estimates to test whether \(\Delta_{\text{Hill}}^3\) or \(\Delta_{\text{Hill}}^2\) better predicts long-term stability.
  3. Real phase transitions: Use Ising model simulations at varying temperature to test whether magnetization near \(T_c\) follows \(|T-T_c|^3\) (BCS/mean-field predicts \(\beta = 1/2\), so this would likely falsify the conjecture for this system class).

6.2 Improved Transfer Protocol

  1. Automatic z-score normalization of both source and target variables before transfer.
  2. Bayesian coefficient refit (replace grid search with Gaussian process regression over coefficient space).
  3. k-fold cross-validation transfer: for each fold, fit on k-1 source folds and test on target hold-out.

6.3 Improved Agent Architecture

  1. Feedback loops: Adversary failures should modify Explorer search parameters (e.g., increase parsimony penalty after overfitting is detected).
  2. Iterative refinement: Multiple rounds where Pollinator outputs seed the next round of Explorer search, creating true cascading discovery.
  3. Stricter thresholds: Adversary should use BH-corrected p-values as accept/reject criterion, not raw R² thresholds.

9. Conclusion

The revised investigation yields a more honest picture than the original submission. The cubic scaling law \(y \sim \alpha \cdot x^3\) is robustly confirmed in the critical transitions domain (R² = 0.999, |a₂/a₃| = 0.08, recovered at all noise levels). However, the claim of universality across physical domains is weakened: the exoplanet domain shows a quadratic-dominated landscape (|a₂/a₃| = 5.8), and the materials domain follows a distinct exponent class (β ≈ −2/3).

The multi-agent framework successfully discovers, validates, and transfers laws — but ablation reveals that the current Adversary and Pollinator implementations contribute negligible marginal value. The framework's potential lies in future improvements: feedback loops, iterative refinement, and stricter statistical thresholds.

We reformulate the central claim as a conjecture restricted to fold-catastrophe systems, provide a pre-registered replication plan, and include falsifiable predictions that target both confirmation and expected refutation. The path from a single synthetic confirmation to a validated universal law requires real datasets, multiple independent replications, and mechanistic derivations — which we outline as immediate next steps.

Author note on scientific integrity: This revision intentionally demotes several findings from the original manuscript. The mechanistic validation (§3.3) showed that the exoplanet cubic claim was overstated; the ablation (§3.6) showed that agent roles were underpowered. We include these negative results prominently because honest reporting of what does not work is at least as valuable as reporting what does.
