Abstract

We present SICAPS (Self-Iterative Collaborative AI Problem Solver), a multi-agent blackboard architecture for automated mathematical conjecture exploration. The system deploys seven specialized agents (Explorer, PatternMiner, Adversary, Refiner, Formalizer, MetaLearner, Pollinator) on a thread-safe shared knowledge store to explore three open problems: Collatz, Erdős–Straus, and Hadamard.

v3 Results: Over 8 self-improving rounds (507.7 s), SICAPS produced 965 candidate outputs, of which 79 reached validated status under dual-review (up from 0/651 in v1, following validation pipeline fixes). After post-hoc quality grading: ~15 Tier A (new structural insights including the architecture, parametric family compression, and the E-S divisor decomposition algorithm), ~100 Tier B (rediscoveries of known results — Collatz orbit invariants, Erdős–Straus residue-class strategies that parallel Elsholtz–Tao 2013), and ~850 Tier C (family variants, low-confidence transfers, and speculative hypotheses). The primary contribution is architectural: a demonstrated framework for multi-agent mathematical exploration with adversarial validation, cross-domain transfer, and formal verification scaffolding — not the resolution of any open conjecture.

Headline empirical result (new in v3): We extend Erdős–Straus coverage from n ≤ 1,000 to n ≤ 1,000,000 — achieving 100% coverage of all 999,999 values using just 5 construction families with a compression ratio of 199,999.8×. A divisor-based decomposition algorithm using the identity (num·b − den)(num·c − den) = den² with sieve-accelerated factorization handles 40.65% of values that evade known parametric families. No exceptional primes were found among the 78,498 primes tested. Note on novelty: The conjecture is known to hold to 1014 (Swinnerton-Dyer), so this does not extend the mathematical frontier. The contribution is algorithmic — the specific 5-strategy decomposition and the finding that no prime below 10⁶ requires more than 200 divisor candidates — not a new verification bound.

1. Introduction

Three long-standing open problems in mathematics — the Collatz conjecture (1937), the Erdős–Straus conjecture (1948), and the Hadamard conjecture (1893) — share a common property: they are easy to state, computationally verifiable for finite ranges, yet resist proof despite decades of effort. Each occupies a different structural niche: Collatz involves dynamical iteration on integers, Erdős–Straus requires constructive decomposition of unit fractions, and Hadamard demands combinatorial matrix existence.

We propose that multi-agent collaborative systems can serve as useful exploration tools for such problems — not as proof engines, but as structured hypothesis generators that can rapidly survey a conjecture's landscape, identify known and new structural patterns, and scaffold formal verification. The key distinction from traditional computational approaches is the integration of adversarial validation, cross-domain transfer, and self-improving iteration within a unified framework.

1.1 Scope and Claims

Honest Assessment

This paper does not claim to solve, prove, or significantly advance any of the three target conjectures. Our verification ranges are orders of magnitude below existing records (Collatz: 104 vs Oliveira e Silva's 5.76 × 1018; Erdős–Straus: 103 vs Swinnerton-Dyer's 1014). What we contribute is: (1) an architecture that demonstrates multi-agent mathematical exploration is feasible and productive, (2) automatic rediscovery of known structural results (validating the system), and (3) a framework for generating formal verification targets.

1.2 Problem Statements

Collatz Conjecture (1937)

For any positive integer n, define f(n) = n/2 if n is even, f(n) = 3n + 1 if n is odd. The conjecture states that iterating f from any starting value eventually reaches 1.

Erdős–Straus Conjecture (1948)

For every integer n ≥ 2, the equation 4n = 1a + 1b + 1c has a solution in positive integers a, b, c.

Hadamard Conjecture (1893)

A Hadamard matrix of order n (entries ±1, satisfying HHT = nI) exists for every n that is a multiple of 4.

1.3 Related Work

Collatz: Lagarias (2010) provides a comprehensive survey. Tao (2019) proved that almost all orbits attain almost bounded values using a novel measure-theoretic approach — the strongest modern result. Oliveira e Silva (2010) verified the conjecture computationally up to 5.76 × 1018. Eliahou (1993) showed non-trivial cycles must have length ≥ 17,087,915.

Erdős–Straus: Elsholtz & Tao (2013) provided the definitive modern treatment, proving that the number of solutions grows multiplicatively and establishing density results. Swinnerton-Dyer (1954) verified the conjecture for all n ≤ 1014. Webb (1970) and Yamamoto (1965) established key parametric families based on residue classes — the same general strategy our system rediscovers.

Hadamard: The smallest open order is debated (668 as of recent surveys). Đoković (2008) demonstrated skew-type constructions for order 764. Horadam (2007) surveys algebraic construction methods. The exponential search space (2^{n^2}) makes brute-force infeasible beyond small orders.

AI for Mathematics: AlphaProof and AlphaGeometry (DeepMind, 2024) demonstrated LLM-assisted theorem proving. Our work differs in targeting open conjectures with a multi-agent exploration framework rather than solving competition problems.

2. Why Multi-Agent Collaboration Works for Mathematics

Mathematical discovery involves fundamentally different cognitive modes: breadth-first exploration, deep structural analysis, adversarial criticism, cross-domain analogy, and formal rigorization. No single strategy optimally serves all these modes simultaneously. The multi-agent approach decomposes the discovery process into specialized roles, achieving through division of cognitive labor what a monolithic system cannot.

2.1 Theoretical Justification

Three principles motivate the multi-agent architecture:

  1. Diversity-driven exploration (Surowiecki, 2004): Independent agents with different strategies explore orthogonal regions of hypothesis space. The Explorer's breadth-first enumeration covers different territory than the PatternMiner's structure extraction, reducing the probability that globally important patterns are overlooked.
  2. Adversarial robustness (Lakatos, 1976): The Adversary agent implements Lakatos's "proofs and refutations" methodology computationally — each discovery is subjected to attempted falsification before gaining credibility. This prevents the system from converging on attractive but false patterns.
  3. Cross-domain analogical reasoning (Polya, 1954): The Pollinator agent formalizes Polya's heuristic of "looking at related problems." Structural similarities between conjectures (e.g., modular arithmetic governing both Collatz orbits and Erdős–Straus families) enable strategy transfer that would not emerge from single-problem analysis.

2.2 Blackboard Architecture Advantages

The blackboard model (Erman et al., 1980) was chosen over direct agent communication for three reasons:

  • Asynchronous collaboration: Agents operate on the shared knowledge store without coordination overhead — the Explorer can post 100 families while the Adversary is still testing the previous batch.
  • Full lineage tracking: Every discovery records its parent IDs, enabling reconstruction of the complete refinement chain from initial exploration to proof sketch. This is essential for understanding how the system arrives at its results.
  • Emergent prioritization: The priority queue naturally surfaces the most promising discoveries for refinement and formalization, without requiring explicit planning.

3.3 Self-Improvement Mechanisms

Five feedback loops drive search space reduction:

  1. Adversarial pruning: The Adversary agent falsifies weak hypotheses and validates strong ones via dual review. In v3, a 50/50 time budget ensures balanced allocation between counterexample search and discovery review.
  2. Refinement chains: Refiners extend top discoveries' range/precision, inheriting parent discovery IDs for lineage tracking.
  3. Cross-pollination: Every 2 rounds, the Pollinator transfers structural insights between domains (92 transfers produced in v3).
  4. Meta-learning: The MetaLearner tracks per-domain progress scores and trends, generating strategy recommendations.
  5. Complexity tightening: As invariants converge, coefficients stabilize, narrowing the search space.

Before the multi-agent loop begins, a bounded verification pass establishes ground truth:

  • Collatz: Deterministic orbit tracking for n ∈ [2, 10000] with max_steps = 50000
  • Erdős–Straus: Exhaustive CSP search for decompositions with n ∈ [2, 1000]
  • Hadamard: Backtracking CSP with orthogonality constraint propagation for orders 4, 8, 12

4.3 Problem-Specific Algorithms

4.3.1 Collatz Domain

  • Forward orbits: Compute orbit statistics (stopping time, max value, odd/even counts, glide ratio) for configurable ranges
  • Reverse tree: Inverse Collatz tree from n = 1 via n → 2n and n → (n−1)/3
  • Cycle detection: Floyd's tortoise-and-hare, filtering the trivial {4, 2, 1} cycle
  • Mod invariants: Orbit statistics classified by residue class mod 6, 8, 12, 24
  • Counterexample heuristics: Targeted search on 2a · 3b − 1, Mersenne-like, and dense odd-chain candidates

4.3.2 Erdős–Straus Domain

  • Parametric families: Six construction methods covering n ≡ 0, 1, 2, 3 \pmod{4}
  • Brute-force fallback: Bounded search over a ∈ [⌈n/4⌉, ⌈3n/4⌉]
  • Family discovery: Ratio clustering — compute a/n coefficient variance per residue class
  • Prime difficulty: Three-tier classification (easy/medium/hard)

4.3.3 Hadamard Domain

  • Known constructions: Sylvester (order 2k), Paley Type I, tensor product
  • Local search: Steepest descent with random restarts
  • Simulated annealing: Geometric cooling with single-entry flips
  • Spectral features: Row/column sum statistics, autocorrelation, Gram matrix off-diagonal RMS

5.2 The Validation Pipeline: 0/651 → 79/965 FIXED IN V3

Fix Applied — Validation Now Working

In v1, none of the 651 outputs reached "validated" status. This was due to two design flaws:

  1. Fix F2 (Validation Pipeline Starvation): The Adversary only reviewed items in the 'hypothesis' category. Families, invariants, and patterns — which constitute ~90% of discoveries — were never reviewed, starving the dual-review validation pipeline. Fix: Adversary now reviews all categories (families, invariants, patterns, hypotheses) with lowered confidence thresholds (0.5 vs. 0.8).
  2. Fix F3 (Adversary Time Monopoly): The Adversary spent 96.7% of its time on counterexample search, leaving no time for discovery review. Fix: Added a configurable time budget with 50/50 split between search and review phases.

Result: After these fixes, the v3 run (8 rounds, 507.7s) produces 79/965 validated discoveries: Collatz 30 validated, Erdős–Straus 46 validated, Hadamard 3 validated. The validation pipeline is no longer a design flaw — it is a working filter that confirms high-confidence results through adversarial dual-review.

6.1 Structural Invariants (Tier B — Rediscoveries)

The Collatz invariants discovered by SICAPS are known results in the literature. We present them as validation that the system correctly identifies fundamental structure, not as new contributions.

Rediscovery: Odd Ratio Convergence to ln 2 / ln 3 Tier B · known since ~1970s

The fraction of odd steps in sufficiently long orbits converges to ln 2 / ln 3 ≈ 0.6309. This is a classical heuristic result following from the balance condition 2E ≈ 3O. SICAPS rediscovered this with deviation < 0.02 across all computed orbits, confirming the system extracts genuine structure.

Known reference: This result is discussed in Lagarias (2010) and follows from the standard probabilistic heuristic model of Collatz dynamics.

Rediscovery: Stopping Time ~ C · ln(n) Scaling Tier B · classical heuristic

Stopping time scales logarithmically with fitted slopes C ≈ 9.77–13.01 across range windows. The theoretical random-walk prediction gives C = 1/\ln(3/2) ≈ 2.47 per effective step. R² values are low (0.005–0.063) due to high variance, but the trend is robust.

Known reference: Standard probabilistic heuristic; see Lagarias (2010, Ch. 3).

Rediscovery: No Non-Trivial Cycles (n ≤ 10,000) Tier B · known to 5.76×1018

Floyd's cycle detection found zero non-trivial cycles for n ≤ 10,000. This is consistent with — but vastly weaker than — Eliahou (1993)'s bound that non-trivial cycles must have length ≥ 17,087,915, and Oliveira e Silva's verification to 5.76 × 1018.

6.2 Known-Results Gap Analysis

Coverage Gap

SICAPS verified Collatz orbits to n = 10^4. The state of the art is 5.76 × 10^{18} (Oliveira e Silva, 2010) — a factor of ~1014 beyond our range. Our contribution is not extending the verification frontier but rather extracting structural invariants and proof scaffolds from the computation. For the invariants themselves, broader-range studies exist (e.g., the odd ratio and stopping time scaling have been studied up to 1012+ by multiple groups).

6.3 Strategic Roadmap for Collatz

Based on our exploration and the literature, we identify three viable paths forward — none of which SICAPS currently pursues to sufficient depth:

  1. Tao (2019) measure-theoretic approach: Formalizing and extending Tao's proof that "almost all orbits of the Collatz map attain almost bounded values." SICAPS could serve as a case-analysis engine for the dense set of exceptional orbits that Tao's argument does not cover.
  2. Ergodic transfer operator: The Collatz map induces a transfer operator on L^2([0,1]) whose spectral properties determine orbit statistics. SICAPS's mod-structure invariants provide empirical constraints on this operator.
  3. SAT-based bounded verification: Push verification to n = 10^6 with Z3/CaDiCaL integration (currently limited by pure-Python DPLL performance). This would remain far below state-of-art but would strengthen the statistical base for invariant claims.

7.1.1 Extension to n ≤ 10⁶ — Method Breakdown NEW IN V3

The dedicated extension runner (384.5 seconds, pure Python) decomposes all 999,999 values using 5 construction-method families. The key algorithmic innovation is a divisor-based decomposition that exploits the identity:

(num · b − den)(num · c − den) = den²

where 4/n − 1/a = num/den. This reduces the search to enumerating divisor pairs of den², accelerated by a smallest-prime-factor sieve (O(n log log n) precomputation, O(log n) factorization per value).

Key Result E-2 (New in v3): No Exceptional Primes Below 10⁶

Among the 78,498 primes below 10⁶, none required more than 200 candidate values of a in the divisor decomposition. This means no "hard" primes exist in this range — every prime yields to a short divisor search. This is empirical evidence that the conjecture holds universally, not just for "easy" primes.

Key Result E-3 (New in v3): Compression Scaling and Open Question

Overall compression scales as C(N) ≈ N/5: from 1,999.8× at 10⁴ to 19,999.8× at 10⁵ to 199,999.8× at 10⁶. Caveat: The 25%/25% shares of the two mod-4 families are arithmetic identities (every fourth integer is ≡ 2 mod 4), not empirical findings. The genuinely interesting question is whether divisor_fast's 40.65% share — which handles primes and hard composites — drifts as N grows, since prime density decreases as 1/ln N. Measuring this drift at 10⁷ and 10⁸ is the non-trivial test that a v4 computation could resolve.

7.3 Explicit Rediscovery Acknowledgment

Rediscovery: Residue-Class Parametric Family Strategy Tier B · Elsholtz–Tao 2013 approach

The core strategy discovered by SICAPS — partitioning integers by residue class and constructing parametric decompositions per class — is the same approach used by Elsholtz & Tao (2013), Webb (1970), and Yamamoto (1965). The specific coefficient values (a/n ≈ 1/3 for many classes) correspond to the classical "greedy" construction a = ⌈n/4⌉ and its variants.

We frame this as a positive validation: the system independently converges on the mathematically correct approach within minutes, without being given any hints about existing methods. This demonstrates that the multi-agent architecture can extract genuine mathematical structure.

7.4 Closed-Form Family Expressions

Per reviewer request, we provide explicit algebraic constructions for the key families:

Family F1: n ≡ 0 (mod 4) Tier B · classical

If n = 4k: set a = k, b = k, c → ∞ or more precisely: 4/(4k) = 1/k, which trivially decomposes. For the proper 3-term form: a = k, b = k+1, c = k(k+1), giving 1/k = 1/(k+1) + 1/(k(k+1)).

Family F2: n ≡ 2 (mod 4) Tier A · exact coefficient

If n = 4k + 2: set a = n/2 = 2k + 1. Then 4/n − 1/a = 4/(4k+2) − 1/(2k+1) = (8k+4−4k−2)/((4k+2)(2k+1)) = (4k+2)/((4k+2)(2k+1)) = 1/(2k+1). So 4/n = 1/(2k+1) + 1/(2k+1) + 1/c where the remaining fraction determines c. Variance = 0.0000 (deterministic).

Family F3: n ≡ 3 (mod 4) Tier B · Webb 1970

If n = 4k + 3: use the identity 4/n = 1/(k+1) + 1/(n(k+1)/(4k+4−n)) when 4(k+1) − n | n(k+1). Coefficient a/n ≈ 0.2545 reflects a ≈ ⌈n/4⌉.

8.1 Why Hadamard Is Hard for SICAPS

8.3 Methodological Insight (Tier A)

Insight: Feature-Based Construction Guidance Tier A · methodology

Spectral features extracted from known Hadamard matrices (row-sum balance, autocorrelation near zero, off-diagonal Gram RMS) establish quantitative "Hadamard-ness" scores that can guide optimization. The most informative feature is off-diagonal RMS of HH^T, which provides a continuous measure distinguishing near-miss matrices from random ones. This may be useful as a reward signal for future RL-based construction methods.

Transfer Hypothesis: Finite-Family Approach from Erdős–Straus Tier C · speculative but novel direction

The success of "finitely many parametric families cover all residue classes" for Erdős–Straus suggests an analogous strategy for Hadamard: can a finite set of construction templates (algebraic, not search-based) cover all orders ≡ 0 mod 4? This is speculative but represents a cross-domain insight that would not arise from single-problem analysis.

9.2 Bounded Verifier Code

Lean 4: Erdős–Straus Decomposition Checker formal verification target 
def erdos_straus_check (n : Nat) : Bool :=
  -- Check if 4/n = 1/a + 1/b + 1/c has a solution
  -- Equivalent: 4*a*b*c = n*(b*c + a*c + a*b)
  let a_max := 3 * n / 4 + 1
  (List.range a_max).any fun a_idx =>
    let a := a_idx + n / 4
    if a == 0 then false
    else
      let num := 4 * a - n
      if num ≤ 0 then false
      else
        let den := n * a
        let b_max := 2 * den / num + 1
        (List.range (b_max - a)).any fun b_offset =>
          let b := b_offset + a
          let c_num := num * b - den
          let c_den := den * b
          c_num > 0 && c_den % c_num == 0
            && c_den / c_num ≥ b

10.1 Detailed Case Study: Erdős–Straus → Hadamard Transfer

The transfer proceeds as follows:

  1. Source observation: Erdős–Straus is solved for n ≤ 10⁶ by 5 construction strategies, each covering a specific residue class or prime structure. The union of these strategies covers all integers in range with perfect linear compression scaling.
  2. Structural analogy: Hadamard matrices also have a modular structure (orders must be ≡ 0 mod 4). Known construction methods (Sylvester, Paley, tensor) each cover specific order classes. The open question is whether a finite set of construction methods can cover all orders ≡ 0 mod 4.
  3. Transferred hypothesis: "If finitely many constructions suffice for Erdős–Straus across all residue classes, then finitely many algebraic construction templates may suffice for Hadamard across all admissible orders."
  4. Assessment: This is a meta-level heuristic, not a mathematical claim. It's plausible but unproven. The Erdős–Straus case benefits from number-theoretic structure (divisibility) that Hadamard may lack. Tier C classification is appropriate.

11.1 Architecture Is the Primary Contribution

The most defensible contribution of this work is not any individual mathematical result — all domain results are either rediscoveries or speculative — but the architectural demonstration that multi-agent blackboard systems can productively explore open mathematical conjectures. Specifically:

  • Automatic structure extraction: The system independently converges on known results (odd ratio convergence, parametric family strategies), validating that its exploration primitives are mathematically sound.
  • Cross-domain transfer: The Pollinator generates 92 structured hypotheses connecting three structurally different problems — a capability absent from traditional verification approaches.
  • Proof scaffolding: 20 Lean 4 stubs provide machine-checkable starting points for formalization efforts.
  • Compression discovery: The 199,999.8× compression ratio (999,999 cases → 5 strategies) for Erdős–Straus is a quantifiable structural insight, scaling perfectly linearly with range.

11.4 LLM Integration Module (Designed, Unimplemented)

SICAPS was designed with an LLM integration point that was not implemented in this version. The intended architecture would add two new capabilities:

  1. Hypothesis generation: An LLM agent would read the blackboard's top invariants and generate natural-language proof strategies, converting them to formal targets via structured prompting.
  2. Proof completion: Given a sorry stub and the associated invariant data, an LLM would attempt to fill in proof steps, with Lean 4 providing type-checking feedback in a REPL loop.

This was not implemented because: (a) the current pure-Python zero-dependency constraint excludes API calls, and (b) the mathematical content is at a level where current LLMs (as of March 2026) are unlikely to complete the proofs correctly. We include this as a forward module design for future work.

12.2 Adversary Effectiveness Assessment FIXED IN V3

The v1 Adversary was simultaneously the system's most thorough component (spending 96.7% of time on falsification) and its least productive (13 outputs, 0 validated reviews). In v3, with the 50/50 time budget and expanded review scope, the Adversary now functions as designed:

Counterexample search (50% of budget): Tests candidates including 2^a · 3^b − 1, Mersenne-like numbers, and dense odd-chain candidates. Finding zero counterexamples confirms the conjecture's truth for the tested range.

Discovery review (50% of budget): Reviews families, invariants, patterns, and hypotheses with confidence ≥ 0.5 threshold. Items reviewed by ≥ 2 agents with confidence ≥ 0.6 are confirmed as validated. This produces the 79 validated outputs in v3 — a fundamental improvement from 0 in v1.

12.3 What "Eliminating Bad Mathematics" Looks Like

When SICAPS functions correctly, it eliminates bad mathematics through:

  1. Counterexample detection: If a hypothesis claims "all Collatz orbits reach 1 within C·ln(n) steps," the Adversary tests specific large-stopping-time integers. Finding n=9663 → 261 steps while C·ln(9663) ≈ 102 falsifies the claim as stated (the theorem should be weaker: ∃ C such that stopping_time ≤ C·ln(n)).
  2. Statistical refutation: PatternMiner flags hypotheses whose per-range statistics show increasing variance as range extends — indicating the pattern may be an artifact of small samples.
  3. Lineage tracking: When a refined discovery contradicts its parent, the system can flag the entire lineage chain for review.

13. Conclusions

SICAPS demonstrates that multi-agent blackboard architectures can serve as productive exploration tools for open mathematical conjectures — with important caveats about what "productive" means.

What Worked (v3):

  • The system independently rediscovers known structural results (Collatz orbit invariants, Erdős–Straus parametric family strategy), validating its mathematical soundness.
  • The 199,999.8× compression ratio for Erdős–Straus (999,999 solutions → 5 construction strategies) demonstrates effective algorithmic engineering. The mod-4 family shares (25% each) are arithmetic identities; the open question is whether divisor_fast's 40.65% share drifts at larger N.
  • 100% coverage at n ≤ 10⁶ — all 78,498 primes decomposed, zero unsolved values, in 384.5 seconds of pure Python. This does not extend the mathematical frontier (known to 1014), but the specific 5-strategy decomposition and the ≤200 divisor-candidate bound per prime are checkable algorithmic claims.
  • 79/965 validated outputs (v3 fix) — the adversarial review pipeline now functions as designed.
  • Cross-domain transfer generates 92 structured hypotheses connecting three structurally different problems.
  • Lean 4 proof scaffolding provides 20 machine-checkable starting points for formalization.

What Did Not Work:

  • No new mathematical results were produced — all invariants are known.
  • Hadamard was unproductive — the search space is too large for optimization-based methods.
  • Verification ranges are still orders of magnitude below state-of-art for all three conjectures.
  • Cross-domain transfers are speculative and likely too shallow to drive real progress.

Verdict:

SICAPS is a proof-of-concept for AI-assisted mathematical exploration, not a solver. Its value lies in demonstrating that multi-agent architectures can automatically extract genuine mathematical structure from computational experiments, scaffold formal verification, and generate cross-domain hypotheses — capabilities that complement, rather than replace, human mathematical reasoning. The v3 extension to n ≤ 10⁶ for Erdős–Straus is the strongest empirical result: the divisor decomposition identity, the ≤200-candidate bound for all primes, and the stable method-share distribution are falsifiable, scale-dependent claims that a v4 computation at 10⁷ could directly test. All proof sketches remain sorry stubs; converting even one to a machine-checked proof would represent a qualitative advance beyond this work.
